## Stress and Burnout Patterns

Burnout looks different for each type, and it often gets misdiagnosed because the symptoms don't match the standard "exhausted and disengaged" template. Here's what to watch for, and what to do when you see it.

**Type 1:** Burnout looks like relentless self-criticism and resentment that leaks into every interaction. The warning sign: everything feels wrong and no one is trying hard enough. Grace, our project manager, starts sending emails at midnight with increasingly sharp feedback. She's not suddenly mean; she's exhausted and her inner critic has taken the wheel. *What helps:* Tell her explicitly that her standards are valued. Then give her permission to let something be "good enough" this one time. She won't believe you the first time. Say it again.

**Type 2:** Burnout looks like martyrdom and emotional collapse. The warning sign: you're tracking every favor you've done and no one has reciprocated. Andre stops being warm and becomes brittle. He says things like, "I guess I'm the only one who cares about this team." *What helps:* Don't argue. Don't explain. Ask him, "What do you need right now?" The question itself may bring him to tears because nobody has asked in months.

**Type 3:** Burnout looks like emptiness behind the achievements. The warning sign: you've hit every goal and feel nothing. Wei delivers her quarterly results and immediately opens her laptop instead of celebrating. She can't stop because stopping means sitting with the void underneath the productivity. *What helps:* Create moments of genuine human connection that are completely decoupled from performance. Lunch with no agenda. A conversation about something that has nothing to do with work.

**Type 4:** Burnout looks like deep withdrawal and hopelessness. The warning sign: they've stopped creating and everything feels pointless. Soren cancels meetings, stops sharing work, and his Slack status stays gray for days. *What helps:* Don't try to cheer him up. Don't say "It's not that bad." Instead, acknowledge the difficulty genuinely: "This sounds really hard. I'm here when you want to talk about it." Then follow up in a few days without waiting for him to reach out. Fours in withdrawal need someone to come to them.

**Type 5:** Burnout looks like total detachment and cynicism. The warning sign: they've stopped caring about the work entirely and their contributions become mechanical. Yumi starts saying "Whatever you want" in meetings, which is her way of announcing she's checked out. *What helps:* Reduce demands on her energy. Give her a smaller, more focused scope. Ask what she's genuinely interested in. A Five who gets to go deep on one thing they care about can recover faster than one who's spread thin across ten things they don't.

**Type 6:** Burnout looks like spiraling anxiety and paralysis. The warning sign: they can't make a decision without imagining catastrophe, and their contingency planning has become the work itself. Darnell starts every sentence with "But what if..." and can't sign off on anything. *What helps:* Don't tell him to "just relax." Instead, reduce uncertainty concretely. Give him clear guardrails: "Here's exactly what happens if this doesn't work, and here's the backup plan." Structure calms a Six faster than reassurance.

**Type 7:** Burnout looks like frantic distraction and inability to be present. The warning sign: they're planning their next escape before the current project is done, and their usual enthusiasm has a manic edge. Lucia is pitching a new initiative every day and getting irritated when anyone asks about the old ones. *What helps:* Gently name the pattern: "I notice you're generating a lot of new ideas. Can we finish one thing before we start another?" Then make the finishing feel rewarding. Sevens aren't allergic to completion; they're allergic to the emptiness they expect on the other side of it.

**Type 8:** Burnout looks like rage and isolation. The warning sign: everyone around them feels like an obstacle or a threat, and their usual directness has hardened into hostility. Marcus stops delegating and starts doing everything himself because "nobody else can handle it." *What helps:* Match his directness. Don't tiptoe. Say, "You're burning out and it's affecting the team. What do you need to not do right now?" An Eight will respect honesty more than any amount of careful concern.

**Type 9:** Burnout looks like numbness and complete disengagement. The warning sign: they've stopped having opinions about anything, even things that matter to them, and they're physically present but energetically absent. Hana nods in every meeting but couldn't tell you what was discussed five minutes later. *What helps:* Don't ask "Are you okay?" (she'll say yes). Instead, ask a specific question that requires her actual opinion: "Hana, I want your honest take on this decision. Not what you think we want to hear, what *you* think." Then wait. The silence is where her opinion lives.
