## Stress and Burnout Patterns

Burnout looks different for each type, and it often gets misdiagnosed because the symptoms don't match the standard "exhausted and disengaged" template. A burned-out Three looks like a top performer. A burned-out Nine looks like a quiet team player. A burned-out Seven looks like the most enthusiastic person in the room. If you only look for the textbook signs of burnout, you will miss most of it.

What follows is a fuller picture: how burnout builds for each type, the difference between early warning signs and full burnout, common mistakes managers make when trying to help, and a specific recovery practice for each.

### Type 1: The Reformer

**The scenario.** Grace is a project manager who cares deeply about doing things right. For months, she has been carrying the team's quality standards on her back, reviewing every deliverable, catching errors nobody else notices, staying late to fix problems she did not create. She tells herself this is just being responsible. But lately her emails have taken on an edge. Feedback that used to be constructive has become cutting. She sent a message at midnight last Tuesday pointing out a formatting error in a document nobody outside the team will ever see. Her team is walking on eggshells. Grace is not suddenly mean. She is exhausted, and her inner critic has taken the wheel.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: increasing rigidity about small details, sighing and eye-rolling in meetings, comments like "If I don't do it, nobody will." Full burnout: relentless resentment that leaks into every interaction, inability to let anything go, physical symptoms like jaw clenching and insomnia, and a growing sense that everything and everyone is falling short.

**What NOT to do.** Do not tell Grace to "lower her standards" or "stop caring so much." To a One, this translates as: "Your commitment to quality is a problem." It confirms her fear that nobody else cares enough, and she will double down. Do not give vague feedback like "You seem stressed." She will hear that as another thing she is doing wrong.

**Recovery practice.** Tell Grace explicitly that her standards are valued. Be specific: "The way you caught that data error last month saved us a week of rework." Then give her permission to let something be "good enough" this one time. Frame it as strategic, not lazy: "For this deadline, what is the minimum quality bar that protects the team?" She will not believe you the first time. Say it again. Then help her delegate one thing she has been holding, and do not let her take it back.

### Type 2: The Helper

**The scenario.** Drew is the person everyone goes to. He remembers birthdays. He covers for people who are running behind. He mentors two junior employees on his own time. He volunteered to organize the team retreat. Nobody asked him to do any of this, and he would never dream of saying no. But this quarter, something has shifted. Drew's warmth has become brittle. He makes comments like "I guess I'm the only one who cares about this team." He offered to help a colleague with a presentation, then complained about it to three other people. He is tracking every favor he has done and calculating who owes him. The ledger in his head is full, and nobody is paying up.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: overextending without being asked, subtle complaints about being underappreciated, difficulty saying no even to unreasonable requests. Full burnout: emotional collapse, passive-aggressive score-keeping, sudden withdrawal of warmth (which shocks the team because it seems to come out of nowhere), and physical exhaustion from pouring out energy that was never replenished.

**What NOT to do.** Do not argue with his perception. Saying "But we do appreciate you" will feel dismissive because Drew's need is not for words; it is for someone to reciprocate care with action. Do not just pile on more gratitude without changing the dynamic. And do not ignore the passive aggression, because the resentment underneath it will only grow.

**Recovery practice.** Ask Drew directly: "What do you need right now?" The question itself may bring him to tears because nobody has asked in months. Then follow through. If he says he needs a lighter load, actually take something off his plate. Help him practice saying no to one request this week. Frame it as self-care, not selfishness: "You taking care of yourself is how you take care of this team long-term."

### Type 3: The Achiever

**The scenario.** Megan delivers her quarterly results and immediately opens her laptop. Her manager suggests the team celebrate. Megan smiles, says "That would be great," and then checks her email under the table. She has hit every target this year. She got the promotion. She was mentioned in the all-hands meeting. And she feels nothing. Not satisfaction, not pride, not even relief. Just a low hum of anxiety and the question that never stops: *What's next?* She cannot stop because stopping means sitting with the void underneath the productivity. The results are a wall she builds between herself and the emptiness. If she stops building, the wall comes down.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: inability to enjoy wins, immediately pivoting to the next goal, working longer hours without being asked, emotional flatness masked by high performance. Full burnout: complete disconnection from feelings, relationships becoming transactional, physical symptoms the Three ignores because slowing down is not an option, and a creeping suspicion that none of it matters but being unable to stop anyway.

**What NOT to do.** Do not assume high performance means someone is fine. Threes in burnout look like top performers from the outside. Do not celebrate only their results; this feeds the pattern. And do not tell them to "take a break" without helping them understand what the break is for. A Three who takes a vacation will spend it optimizing the vacation.

**Recovery practice.** Create moments of genuine human connection that are completely decoupled from performance. Lunch with no agenda. A conversation about something that has nothing to do with work. Ask Megan about a movie she watched, a meal she cooked, a walk she took. If she steers it back to work, gently redirect: "I'm not asking about your projects right now. I'm asking about you." The discomfort she feels in that moment is the beginning of recovery.

### Type 4: The Individualist

**The scenario.** Tyler is a UX researcher who used to send long, thoughtful Slack messages about user insights. He would stay after meetings to sketch ideas on the whiteboard. He once redesigned an entire survey instrument because he felt the original missed the emotional nuance of the user experience. That was three months ago. Now Tyler cancels meetings. He has stopped sharing work. His Slack status has been gray for days. When he does show up, his contributions are minimal and tinged with a quiet bitterness. He made a comment last week about the team "not really caring about quality" that landed hard because everyone knew it was not entirely about the team.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: increasing sensitivity to feedback, quiet withdrawal from collaborative spaces, comments about work feeling meaningless, comparison to others' recognition or opportunities. Full burnout: deep withdrawal and hopelessness, loss of creative energy, inability to engage with work they once found meaningful, and a pervasive sense that nothing they contribute matters.

**What NOT to do.** Do not try to cheer Tyler up. "It's not that bad" dismisses his experience and confirms his fear that nobody understands. Do not ignore the withdrawal, hoping he will come back on his own. And do not give him a pep talk about the team's mission; a Four in burnout will hear it as shallow and feel more alienated.

**Recovery practice.** Acknowledge the difficulty genuinely: "This sounds really hard. I've noticed you've been pulling back, and I want you to know I see it." Then follow up in a few days without waiting for him to reach out. Fours in withdrawal need someone to come to them. Give Tyler a project that reconnects him to meaning, something where his particular depth of perception is visibly valued. Not busy work. Real work that only he could do the way he does it.

### Type 5: The Investigator

**The scenario.** Claire is a senior analyst who used to send beautifully structured memos that the entire department relied on. She would ask incisive questions in meetings that reframed problems everyone else had accepted. Now she sits in meetings and says, "Whatever you want." Her memos have become perfunctory. She declines optional meetings. She eats lunch at her desk with the door closed. She is not disengaged because she stopped caring. She is disengaged because she has been drained. Too many meetings, too many demands on her attention, too many requests that required her to give pieces of herself she could not afford to lose. A Five's energy is finite in a way that other types do not always understand. When the account is empty, the Five does not get irritable or emotional. She goes blank.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: increasing withdrawal from optional interactions, shorter responses in communication, declining invitations she would have accepted before, a subtle shift from engaged quiet to detached quiet. Full burnout: total emotional detachment, cynicism about the work, mechanical contributions that lack the insight she is known for, and the phrase "I don't care" used without irony.

**What NOT to do.** Do not respond to her withdrawal by increasing contact. More meetings, more check-ins, more "how are you doing?" conversations will drain her further. Do not interpret her detachment as a personal slight. And do not assign her to a cross-functional task force as a way to "get her re-engaged." A depleted Five needs less, not more.

**Recovery practice.** Reduce demands on Claire's energy. Give her a smaller, more focused scope. Ask what she is genuinely interested in. A Five who gets to go deep on one thing she cares about can recover faster than one who is spread thin across ten things she does not. Send her a brief written message rather than scheduling a meeting: "I want to make sure you have what you need. Is there anything I should change about how we're working?" Then actually change it.

### Type 6: The Loyalist

**The scenario.** Darnell has always been the person who catches what everyone else misses. His risk assessments are thorough. His contingency plans have saved the team more than once. But lately, the scanning has become the work itself. He cannot sign off on the project timeline because he keeps finding new scenarios that need to be addressed. He starts every sentence with "But what if..." His questions, once valuable, now paralyze team discussions. He sent his manager a two-page email at midnight listing fourteen things that could go wrong with a client presentation. His manager is frustrated. Darnell is terrified, and his terror looks exactly like his competence, which is why nobody noticed it was a problem until it was.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: increased questioning of decisions that were already made, difficulty committing to a course of action, requests for more data before proceeding, testing loyalty by asking indirect questions. Full burnout: spiraling anxiety and paralysis, catastrophic thinking that cannot be soothed by reassurance, loss of trust in leadership, and a pervasive sense that nothing is safe.

**What NOT to do.** Do not tell Darnell to "just relax" or "stop worrying." His anxiety is not a choice; it is his nervous system's threat detection operating at full volume. Do not dismiss his concerns as negativity. Many of them are accurate. And do not respond to his questions with impatience; he will interpret it as confirmation that the environment is not safe.

**Recovery practice.** Reduce uncertainty concretely. Give Darnell clear guardrails: "Here is exactly what happens if this doesn't work, and here is the backup plan." Structure calms a Six faster than reassurance. Help him sort his concerns into two categories: things he can influence and things he cannot. For the first category, give him a clear action. For the second, name it honestly: "You're right that this could happen. It's outside our control. Here's how we'll respond if it does." Specificity is the antidote.

### Type 7: The Enthusiast

**The scenario.** Jenna is pitching a new initiative every day. She has three Slack channels for projects she started this quarter and a whiteboard full of ideas for next quarter. Her energy is infectious, but it has a manic edge that was not there before. When someone asks about the status of last month's project, she gets irritated. "That's basically done," she says, though it is not. She volunteers for everything and finishes nothing. She rearranged her workspace twice this week. She booked a conference in another city. She is not being productive. She is running from something, and the running looks exactly like enthusiasm.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: increasing number of new ideas without follow-through on existing ones, restlessness and difficulty being present, reframing problems as opportunities rather than addressing them, impatience with routine tasks. Full burnout: frantic distraction, inability to sit with any negative feeling, irritability when asked to focus or finish, and a growing gap between her cheerful exterior and the anxiety underneath.

**What NOT to do.** Do not just go along with the constant new ideas; it enables the avoidance pattern. Do not kill her energy with blunt constraints, because a Seven who feels caged will either leave or become genuinely depressed. And do not mistake her enthusiasm for engagement. A Seven who is generating ideas at a manic pace is often less engaged, not more.

**Recovery practice.** Gently name the pattern: "I notice you're generating a lot of new ideas. Can we finish one thing before we start another?" Then make the finishing feel rewarding, not punishing. Sevens are not allergic to completion. They are allergic to the emptiness they expect on the other side of it. Help Jenna experience that finishing something can be its own kind of high. Celebrate completed work. And give her a structured way to capture new ideas without acting on them immediately: a "parking lot" list that she reviews monthly instead of daily.

### Type 8: The Challenger

**The scenario.** Marcus has stopped delegating. He comes in early, stays late, and has taken back three tasks he assigned to his team last month. His feedback in meetings has shifted from direct to aggressive. A junior team member asked a question yesterday and Marcus snapped, "Figure it out yourself." He apologized an hour later, but the damage was done. The rest of the team has started routing around him, going to each other instead of bringing issues to Marcus, which makes him feel more isolated, which makes him grip harder. He is carrying everything because "nobody else can handle it," and the weight is turning him into someone his team does not want to be around.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: taking back delegated work, increasing impatience with perceived weakness, longer hours, dismissiveness in meetings. Full burnout: rage and isolation, hostility that replaces directness, inability to trust anyone with responsibility, and a physical toll (headaches, elevated blood pressure, sleep disruption) that the Eight refuses to acknowledge.

**What NOT to do.** Do not tiptoe around Marcus. He will interpret caution as weakness or manipulation, and it will make him angrier. Do not send him to an HR-led "stress management" workshop; he will see it as an insult. And do not ignore the impact on the team, because the Eight's burnout creates a cascade of stress in everyone around him.

**Recovery practice.** Match his directness. Say, "You're burning out and it's affecting the team. What do you need to *not* do right now?" An Eight will respect honesty more than any amount of careful concern. Help him identify one thing he can let go of this week. Frame it as strength, not weakness: "A strong leader builds a team that can operate without him. What are you holding that someone else could carry?" Then check in on whether he actually let it go. Eights agree to delegate and then quietly take it back. Follow through matters.

### Type 9: The Peacemaker

**The scenario.** Lisa nods in every meeting. She agrees with the project plan. She agrees with the timeline change. She agrees with the new client strategy. She agrees that the team should work through the weekend. She has not disagreed with anything in four months. Her manager thinks she is a team player. Her colleagues think she is easygoing. The truth is that Lisa checked out two months ago. She is physically present and energetically absent. She could not tell you what was discussed five minutes after a meeting ends. She goes through her task list mechanically, does adequate work, and goes home to watch television until she falls asleep. She is not resting. She is numb. And the numbness is so familiar that she does not recognize it as burnout.

**Early warning signs vs. full burnout.** Early: fewer unsolicited opinions, increased "going along" with decisions she previously would have questioned, falling behind on tasks without raising a flag, merging with other people's priorities instead of holding her own. Full burnout: complete disengagement, numbness that looks like calm, inability to access her own preferences or opinions, and a flat affect that the team misreads as contentment.

**What NOT to do.** Do not ask "Are you okay?" She will say yes, every time, because saying no requires a confrontation she does not have the energy for. Do not assume that quiet agreement means genuine buy-in. And do not pile on more work because she never complains. Her silence is not capacity. It is surrender.

**Recovery practice.** Ask Lisa a specific question that requires her actual opinion: "Lisa, I want your honest take on this decision. Not what you think we want to hear. What *you* think." Then wait. The silence before a Nine answers is not empty. It is the time it takes for her to locate an opinion that has been buried under everyone else's. When she does speak, take it seriously. If her feedback changes nothing, she will learn that her voice does not matter, and she will stop offering it. Follow through on what she says, and she will start to wake up.
