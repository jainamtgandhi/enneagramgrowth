Every office has its archetypes. The person who stays late perfecting a deck nobody asked for. The one who remembers every birthday but cannot say no. The one who has started four initiatives and finished one. These are not quirks or character flaws. They are Enneagram patterns, running on autopilot, shaping how each person leads, collaborates, and struggles without realizing it.

> **New to the Enneagram?** Start with [the nine types](/types) to get a foundation before diving into workplace patterns.

## How Each Type Shows Up at Work

### Type 1: The Reformer

Picture a project manager named Grace who stays an hour late to reformat a slide deck that was "fine." Her work is thorough and principled, and her team trusts her quality instinctively. When she raises a concern, people listen, because she's usually right. But Grace's strength has a shadow. She sent a colleague back to revise a report four times on a Tuesday, each time pointing out issues that mattered to her but not to the client. Her team respects her standards but dreads her feedback, because "good enough" is a phrase that causes Grace physical discomfort. If you work with a One, know this: their corrections come from love of the work, not contempt for you. But they genuinely may not see the difference from the receiving end.

### Type 2: The Helper

Meet Drew, who remembers every team member's birthday, knows who's going through a divorce, and always has snacks in his desk drawer. He's the relational glue that holds his department together. When a new hire is struggling, Drew is the one who checks in. But Drew also said yes to organizing the offsite, mentoring two interns, and covering a colleague's presentation, all in the same week. By Friday, he's running on fumes and quietly seething that nobody asked how *he's* doing. His blind spot is real: Drew advocates fiercely for everyone except himself, and his resentment leaks out in passive comments that confuse teammates who thought he was happy to help.

### Type 3: The Achiever

Consider a sales director named Megan who hit 140% of quota last quarter and is already strategizing for next quarter before the celebration email goes out. Megan is the engine of her team. She sets goals, hits them, and makes it look effortless. People rally around her vision because her energy is genuinely infectious. But in a team retrospective last month, two quieter colleagues mentioned they felt steamrolled. Megan had cut off their input in a brainstorm because their ideas "weren't going to move the needle." She wasn't being cruel. She was being efficient. But efficiency without inclusion is just speed, and Megan is learning (slowly) that the ideas she dismissed might have been the ones that mattered most.

### Type 4: The Individualist

Picture a designer named Tyler who produces work with a signature quality his clients can't quite articulate but always notice. He sees what's missing in a way that elevates every project he touches. His creative instincts are the reason his team wins pitches. But Tyler took a routine round of design feedback last week and went silent for two days. The feedback was constructive and specific. It didn't matter. To Tyler, feedback on his work feels indistinguishable from feedback on his soul. His withdrawal wasn't dramatic; he just stopped contributing in meetings and responded to Slack messages with one-word answers. If you work with a Four, know that they need you to acknowledge what's working before you discuss what isn't. Not as flattery. As oxygen.

### Type 5: The Investigator

Meet a data analyst named Claire who actually reads the entire report. Every footnote. Every appendix. Her presentations are airtight because she has anticipated every question before it's asked. She brings clarity and calm analysis when everyone else is reacting emotionally. But Claire guards her time like a scarce resource. She declined three meeting invitations last week, not because she was busy, but because she calculated that the meetings wouldn't yield enough value to justify the energy cost. She shares conclusions without showing her work, which makes her seem secretive when she's actually just efficient. If you ambush Claire with a question in a hallway, you'll get a blank stare and a request to "send that in an email." It's not rudeness. It's a Five's need to prepare before they give you their thinking.

### Type 6: The Loyalist

Picture a compliance manager named Darnell who spotted the contract vulnerability that saved his company from a lawsuit. His team calls him "the canary" because he senses danger before anyone else does. His loyalty and follow-through make him the backbone of any team he joins. But Darnell also delayed a product launch by two weeks because he wanted to run one more scenario analysis. His manager was frustrated. The product was ready. But Darnell couldn't sign off until he was sure, and "sure" is a destination he never quite reaches. His need for reassurance can exhaust leaders who mistake it for lack of confidence, when it's actually an excess of responsibility.

### Type 7: The Enthusiast

Consider a brand strategist named Jenna who generates more ideas in a single brainstorm than most people produce in a month. She's the one who sees connections between markets nobody else noticed. Her energy pulls teams out of ruts and into possibility. But Jenna has started four strategic initiatives this quarter and finished one. When the work moves from exciting to tedious (which it always does, eventually), her attention migrates to the next shiny thing. She reframes problems so quickly that her team sometimes realizes, mid-pivot, that they never actually solved the original one. If you manage a Seven, your job is to channel the energy, not suppress it. But you also need to hold the line on completion.

### Type 8: The Challenger

Meet a VP of operations named Marcus who said in a team meeting, "I don't care about your feelings about the restructuring. I care about whether it works." People know where they stand with Marcus. He's the one willing to have the conversation everyone else has been avoiding for months. His directness cuts through politics and gets things moving. But Marcus also spoke over three colleagues in that same meeting, dismissed a junior team member's concern as "not strategic enough," and didn't notice the silence he left in his wake. His blind spot isn't cruelty; it's a genuine inability to see that his volume is turning people off before they can hear his message.

### Type 9: The Peacemaker

Picture a team lead named Lisa who was asked by her director, "What do you think of the new org structure?" Lisa paused, smiled, and said, "I think it could work." She had seventeen thoughts about the org structure, most of them critical. She shared none of them. Lisa is the natural mediator, the one who sees every perspective and creates space for everyone to be heard. But she creates that space partly by vacating her own. She goes along with decisions she disagrees with, avoids conflict until it's too late to address gracefully, and struggles to assert her own priorities until they've been overridden so many times that she erupts, seemingly out of nowhere, and everyone is confused.
