## Conflict Resolution

### Body Center Conflict

**Body Center conflict** is about autonomy and control. Eights confront directly, Ones correct through principles, and Nines withdraw to avoid the whole thing.

Picture this: an Eight director and a One manager disagree about how to handle an underperforming employee. The Eight says, "Fire them. They've had enough chances." The One says, "We need to follow the proper performance improvement process." Neither is wrong. But the Eight hears the One's insistence on process as weakness, and the One hears the Eight's decisiveness as recklessness. Meanwhile, the Nine team lead says nothing, privately agrees with the One, and goes home with a stomachache.

The key: acknowledge the anger underneath. Body types need to feel that their boundaries and autonomy are respected before they can soften. Start with "I hear you" before you start with "But."

### Heart Center Conflict

**Heart Center conflict** is about worth and recognition. Twos feel unappreciated, Threes feel exposed, and Fours feel misunderstood.

Picture this: a Two project coordinator, a Three team lead, and a Four creative director are on the same project. The project succeeds. The Three gets the credit in the company newsletter. The Two, who handled all the logistics that made the project possible, feels invisible. The Four, whose creative concept was the entire reason the project worked, feels like their contribution was reduced to a footnote. The Three doesn't understand why everyone isn't celebrating.

The key: validate the person before addressing the problem. Heart types need to feel seen before they can hear feedback. Saying "Your work mattered and here's specifically how" isn't flattery. It's the door that opens the conversation.

### Head Center Conflict

**Head Center conflict** is about safety and trust. Fives feel invaded, Sixes feel unsupported, and Sevens feel trapped.

Picture this: a Five engineer has been working on an architecture proposal for two weeks. A Six product manager asks to review it before it's "ready." The Five bristles (their work is being judged before it's complete). The Six is confused (they were just trying to stay informed so nothing goes wrong). A Seven designer jumps in with a completely different approach, and the Five feels like their two weeks of work just evaporated.

The key: provide clarity and predictability. Head types need to feel that the situation is understandable and that they have enough information before they can engage constructively. Surprises are not fun for this center. They're threats.
