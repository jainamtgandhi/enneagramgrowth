Most workplace conflict is not about what people think it is about. The stated issue, the missed deadline, the budget disagreement, the meeting that went sideways, is almost always a surface story. Underneath it, each person is protecting something they cannot easily name: their autonomy, their worth, or their sense of safety. The Enneagram makes those hidden stakes visible, which turns conflict from a power struggle into a conversation.

> **Understanding the centers is key here.** If you haven't read [The Three Centers](/library/centers), start there.

## How Each Center Experiences Conflict

### Body Center Conflict (Types 8, 9, 1)

**Body Center conflict** is about autonomy and control. Eights confront directly, Ones correct through principles, and Nines withdraw to avoid the whole thing. The common thread is anger, though it shows up differently for each type. Eights express it openly. Ones channel it into righteous criticism. Nines suppress it until it leaks out as passive resistance or sudden eruption.

Picture this: an Eight director and a One manager disagree about how to handle an underperforming employee. The Eight says, "Fire them. They've had enough chances." The One says, "We need to follow the proper performance improvement process." Neither is wrong. But the Eight hears the One's insistence on process as weakness, and the One hears the Eight's decisiveness as recklessness. Meanwhile, the Nine team lead says nothing, privately agrees with the One, and goes home with a stomachache.

**What the Body Center needs before it can resolve anything:** To feel that their boundaries and autonomy are respected. Body types cannot engage constructively when they feel controlled, overruled, or dismissed. Start with "I hear you" before you start with "But."

**Resolution steps for Body Center conflict:**

1. **Name the autonomy issue directly.** "It sounds like you feel your judgment is being overridden. Is that right?" Body types need to hear that you see the real issue, not just the surface disagreement.
2. **Separate positions from boundaries.** Ask each person: "What is your position on this?" and then: "What boundary would this cross for you?" Often the position is negotiable while the boundary is not.
3. **Give each person a domain.** Body types resolve conflict best when they can each feel authority over something. "You own the decision on timeline. You own the decision on quality standards. Here is where those need to align."
4. **For the Nine in the room:** Before the meeting ends, ask them directly: "What is your honest take?" If they say "I think either approach could work," press gently: "If you had to choose one, which would it be?" Nines will give their real opinion if they feel the question is sincere and the relationship will survive the answer.

### Heart Center Conflict (Types 2, 3, 4)

**Heart Center conflict** is about worth and recognition. Twos feel unappreciated, Threes feel exposed, and Fours feel misunderstood. The common thread is shame, the fear that they are not valued for who they really are. Heart Center conflicts often look like interpersonal drama, but they are almost always about identity.

Picture this: a Two project coordinator, a Three team lead, and a Four creative director are on the same project. The project succeeds. The Three gets the credit in the company newsletter. The Two, who handled all the logistics that made the project possible, feels invisible. The Four, whose creative concept was the entire reason the project worked, feels like their contribution was reduced to a footnote. The Three does not understand why everyone is not celebrating.

**What the Heart Center needs before it can resolve anything:** To feel seen. Heart types cannot engage constructively when they feel invisible, mischaracterized, or reduced. Saying "Your work mattered and here is specifically how" is not flattery. It is the door that opens the conversation.

**Resolution steps for Heart Center conflict:**

1. **Validate before problem-solving.** Acknowledge each person's specific contribution before addressing the conflict. "The logistics you managed were the backbone of this project. The creative direction was what made it special. The leadership that pulled it together was essential." Say it out loud. Heart types need to hear it, not assume it.
2. **Separate the person from the problem.** Heart types internalize conflict as a statement about their value. Make explicit: "This is about the process, not about whether your work matters. Your work matters. Now let's fix the process."
3. **Ask what "being seen" would look like.** For the Two: "What kind of recognition would have felt right?" For the Four: "How should your contribution have been acknowledged?" For the Three: "What are you afraid people think about you right now?" These are uncomfortable questions. They are also the fastest route to resolution, because they surface the actual need underneath the resentment.
4. **Create shared credit structures.** Heart Center conflicts frequently stem from attribution. A simple fix: before any project wraps, build a shared "who did what" document. It sounds bureaucratic. It prevents the kind of invisible contribution resentment that poisons teams.

### Head Center Conflict (Types 5, 6, 7)

**Head Center conflict** is about safety and trust. Fives feel invaded, Sixes feel unsupported, and Sevens feel trapped. The common thread is fear, the sense that the situation has become unpredictable and therefore dangerous. Head Center conflicts often look like intellectual disagreements, but they are almost always about security.

Picture this: a Five engineer has been working on an architecture proposal for two weeks. A Six product manager asks to review it before it is "ready." The Five bristles: their work is being judged before it is complete. The Six is confused: they were just trying to stay informed so nothing goes wrong. A Seven designer jumps in with a completely different approach, and the Five feels like their two weeks of work just evaporated.

**What the Head Center needs before it can resolve anything:** Clarity and predictability. Head types cannot engage constructively when the situation feels chaotic, ambiguous, or emotionally loaded. Surprises are not fun for this center. They are threats.

**Resolution steps for Head Center conflict:**

1. **Slow down and provide context.** Before discussing the conflict itself, explain the process: "Here is what we are going to do. We are going to hear each perspective, then identify the actual disagreement, then decide together. Nobody's work is being thrown out." Head types need to see the structure before they can participate in the content.
2. **Honor preparation.** For the Five: never dismiss work that required deep thought. "Walk us through your thinking" is the most respectful thing you can say. For the Six: "What risks do you see?" channels their analysis into something productive. For the Seven: "What possibilities are we missing?" gives their generative mind a constructive role.
3. **Separate process from decision.** Many Head Center conflicts are actually about process (when to review, who has input, how decisions get made) rather than the decision itself. Name that: "It sounds like the disagreement is less about the architecture and more about when and how input gets incorporated. Can we solve the process question first?"
4. **Put agreements in writing.** Head types are reassured by documentation. After resolving a conflict, send a brief summary: "Here is what we agreed, who owns what, and when we will check in." A Five needs to know their work was valued. A Six needs to know the plan is solid. A Seven needs to know there is room for iteration later.

## Cross-Center Conflict

The most confusing conflicts happen between people from different centers, because each person needs something the other does not intuitively understand.

**Body vs. Heart:** The Eight says, "Just do the work, stop worrying about how people feel." The Two says, "People's feelings are the work." Both are right. The resolution: acknowledge that the project needs both execution and morale, and assign those responsibilities clearly rather than expecting one person to hold both.

**Body vs. Head:** The One says, "We need to decide now and get moving." The Six says, "We need to think through the risks first." Both are right. The resolution: set a time-bounded analysis phase with a decision deadline. "We will spend 48 hours identifying risks. Then we decide and move."

**Heart vs. Head:** The Four says, "This approach has no soul." The Five says, "Soul is not a metric." Both are right. The resolution: translate between the languages. "When you say 'soul,' do you mean it lacks a clear point of view? When you say 'metric,' do you mean it needs a measurable outcome?" Often the disagreement evaporates once each person's concern is restated in the other's language.

## The Five-Minute Conflict Protocol

When tension is live and there is no time for a long process, use this:

1. **Each person answers in writing (2 minutes):** "What am I feeling right now? What do I actually need? What story am I telling myself about the other person?"
2. **Share answers aloud (2 minutes).** No responding yet. Just listening.
3. **One question (1 minute):** "Now that we've heard each other, what is the smallest thing we could change to move forward?"

Most conflicts dissolve, or at least transform, when people hear each other's honest answers to those three questions. The story question is especially powerful: "I was telling myself you didn't respect my expertise" hits very differently from "You never listen to me."
