The most common team dysfunction is not personality conflict. It is the absence of an entire way of seeing. A team of analysts who never check how people feel. A team of relationship-builders who never ship anything. A team of doers who never ask whether they are doing the right thing. The Enneagram's three centers explain why teams get stuck, and what is missing when they do.

> **Need a refresher on the three centers?** Read [The Three Centers](/library/centers) first.

## Team Dynamics by Center

**Body Center teams (8, 9, 1)** are action-oriented. They want to *do* something. Decisions get made quickly, and there's a bias toward movement. Picture a leadership team of an Eight CEO, a One COO, and a Nine head of HR. In practice, the Eight drives decisions at speed, the One immediately starts refining and correcting the execution plan, and the Nine quietly absorbs the tension between them without voicing the objection that could prevent a major misstep. The shadow side: unacknowledged anger simmers beneath the surface. The Eight is openly frustrated, the One is silently resentful, and the Nine is numb. If your team skews Body, build in reflection pauses before big decisions. And create explicit space for the Nines to disagree before they go along with something they'll regret.

**Heart Center teams (2, 3, 4)** are relationally attuned. They care about how people feel, how things look, and whether the team is connected. Imagine a marketing department staffed mostly by Twos, Threes, and Fours. The Twos are making sure everyone feels supported. The Threes are driving toward the campaign deadline. The Fours are advocating for creative integrity. Sounds balanced until the first round of negative feedback arrives. The Twos feel unappreciated ("We did all this work and nobody cares"). The Threes feel exposed ("This makes us look incompetent"). The Fours feel unseen ("Nobody understands what we were going for"). Suddenly the room is thick with unspoken shame, and nobody is addressing the actual feedback. If your team skews Heart, create explicit permission for direct feedback and make it safe to separate work quality from personal worth.

**Head Center teams (5, 6, 7)** are analytical and strategic. They think before they act, anticipate problems, and generate ideas. Now picture a product team with a Five lead engineer, a Six product manager, and a Seven design lead. The Five wants more data before committing to a direction. The Six wants to map out every risk scenario. The Seven wants to pivot to a more exciting approach. A month passes. The whiteboard is covered in frameworks. The Slack channel is full of "what ifs." Nothing has shipped. The shadow side: analysis paralysis. They can overthink, over-plan, and delay action while chasing certainty that never comes. If your team skews Head, set deadlines for decisions, reward action (not just analysis), and appoint someone (ideally a Body type) to call the question when the thinking has gone on long enough.

### The Missing-Center Problem

The most common team dysfunction isn't conflict between types. It's the absence of an entire center. A startup founded by two Fives and a Seven (all Head types) will generate brilliant strategy and zero emotional awareness. They'll lose talent because nobody feels valued. A nonprofit run by Twos and Fours (all Heart types) will have deep mission alignment and terrible execution. They'll plan a fundraiser with beautiful invitations and no budget. When you notice a blind spot in your team, look for the missing center. You don't need to hire for it (though that helps). You just need to consciously include it in your process.
