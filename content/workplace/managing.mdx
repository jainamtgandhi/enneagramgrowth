Every manager eventually discovers that what motivates one person demoralizes another. The feedback style that lights a fire under your top performer sends someone else into a two-day withdrawal. The delegation approach that empowers one team member makes another feel abandoned.

This is not because management is mysterious. It is because people are wired differently, and most management advice treats everyone as the same generic employee. The Enneagram provides a more specific map.

## Managing Each Type

### Type 1: The Reformer

**What motivates them:** Clear standards, quality work, and knowing their effort has made something genuinely better. Ones thrive when they can see that their attention to detail matters. They do not need constant praise, but they need to know their standards are respected.

**How to give feedback:** Lead with what they did well, and be specific. "The structure of this report was excellent" lands better than "Good job." Then be equally specific about what to adjust. Vague criticism like "make it better" or "something feels off" is a One's worst nightmare, because their inner critic will fill the gap with worst-case interpretations. Say exactly what you mean.

**What to avoid:** Public criticism. Ones are already their own harshest judges. Adding external criticism in front of others compounds the shame. Also avoid implying that their standards are excessive. To a One, "You're overthinking this" translates to "Your care about quality is a problem." Try instead: "Your thoroughness is an asset. For this particular deadline, which elements matter most?"

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Increasing rigidity. When a One starts correcting things that do not need correcting, or sending back work for a fourth revision on details that only they notice, they are not being difficult. They are stressed, and their stress response is to tighten control. The fix is not to tell them to relax. It is to help them prioritize.

### Type 2: The Helper

**What motivates them:** Personal connection and the knowledge that their contributions matter to real people. Twos are not motivated by abstract goals. They are motivated by *who* the goal serves. "We need to hit Q3 targets" lands flat. "This project is going to make a real difference for the customer support team" lands deep.

**How to give feedback:** Check in personally first. Ask how they are doing, and mean it. Then deliver the feedback with relational warmth. Twos can hear hard truths from people who have demonstrated they care about them. Frame developmental feedback as investment in the relationship: "I am telling you this because I want you to succeed here, and I see something that might be getting in your way."

**What to avoid:** Taking their helpfulness for granted. The fastest way to lose a Two is to accept their over-extension without ever asking what they need in return. Also avoid giving feedback in a purely transactional way. A Two who hears "your numbers are down" without any relational context will hear "you have failed me personally."

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Martyrdom. When a Two starts making passive comments about how much they do and how little anyone notices, they are past the point of healthy helping. They are keeping score, which means their resentment has been building for weeks. The intervention is simple but powerful: ask them what they need, and then follow through.

### Type 3: The Achiever

**What motivates them:** Clear goals, autonomy, and recognition for results. Set the target, get out of their way, and acknowledge the win. Threes are self-starters who convert ambiguity into action faster than almost any other type. Give them a challenge and they will run at it.

**How to give feedback:** Privately, and framed as strategy rather than judgment. "Here is what would make you even more effective" works. "Here is what you did wrong" does not, because the Three will hear it as an identity statement rather than a performance note. Frame everything as an optimization opportunity. Threes love optimizing.

**What to avoid:** Publicly comparing them unfavorably to someone else. Threes look confident, but their self-worth is wired to performance. Being told they are falling behind someone else does not motivate them; it triggers a shame spiral that they will mask with even more frantic achievement. Also avoid vague goals. "Do your best" is useless to a Three. "Hit 120% of quota by March 15" is fuel.

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Burnout masked as productivity. A Three who is in trouble does not slow down. They speed up. They take on more, work later, deliver more impressively, and appear to be thriving. The tell is in the private moments: irritability, emotional flatness, inability to enjoy their own wins. If a Three hits a major milestone and your first instinct is not celebration but concern because they are already moving to the next thing, pay attention.

### Type 4: The Individualist

**What motivates them:** Meaningful work and creative freedom. Fours need to feel that their contribution matters in a way that goes beyond metrics. They are not motivated by competition or prestige. They are motivated by significance, by the sense that their unique perspective is being valued.

**How to give feedback:** Acknowledge what is working before discussing what is not. This is not flattery. For a Four, hearing that something in their work resonated is emotional oxygen. Without it, any subsequent criticism feels like a total rejection. Then be honest, but personal. "I want to understand your perspective" is a phrase that opens doors with Fours that transactional feedback keeps shut.

**What to avoid:** Treating their emotional reactions as unprofessional. When a Four withdraws after feedback, they are not being dramatic. They are processing, and they process deeply. Chasing them with "Are you okay? Are you okay?" makes it worse. Give them a day, then follow up with genuine curiosity: "I value your thinking on this. Where did you land?" Also avoid assigning busywork. A Four who is stuck doing tasks they find meaningless will disengage faster than any other type.

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Withdrawal and a shift toward melancholy in their communication. A Four who stops contributing ideas in meetings, responds to messages with uncharacteristically short answers, or begins describing their role as "just a job" has emotionally checked out. The path back is not a pep talk. It is a conversation about what would make the work meaningful again.

### Type 5: The Investigator

**What motivates them:** Intellectual challenge, autonomy, and respect for their expertise. Fives want to go deep, not wide. Give them one complex problem rather than five simple tasks, and they will produce work of remarkable quality.

**How to give feedback:** In writing, or at minimum, with advance notice. "Can we schedule 15 minutes tomorrow to discuss the project?" gives a Five time to prepare. Dropping into their office with "Quick question" (which is never a quick question) violates their need for predictability. When you do give feedback, be precise and logical. Fives respect data and specificity. "I noticed three areas where the analysis could be stronger" is received as useful. "This needs more work" is received as an ambush.

**What to avoid:** Mandatory group brainstorms with no agenda. Open-ended social events framed as "team building." Meetings that could have been emails. Each of these drains a Five's limited social energy budget without providing proportional value. Also avoid interpreting their quietness as disinterest. A Five in a meeting who says nothing may be the most engaged person in the room. They are processing. When they do speak, listen carefully, because they have been composing that thought for twenty minutes.

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Increasing withdrawal. Fives who feel overwhelmed do not complain. They disappear. Fewer emails. Shorter meetings. Declining social invitations. If you notice a Five's presence shrinking, do not crowd them. Send a brief, written message: "I want to make sure you have what you need to do your best work. Is there anything I should change?" The question itself is often enough.

### Type 6: The Loyalist

**What motivates them:** Transparency, consistency, and earned trust. Sixes do not trust easily, but once trust is established, they become the most loyal, thorough collaborators on your team. The key is: trust is built through predictability, not charisma.

**How to give feedback:** With context. Always explain the "why" behind the feedback and any resulting changes. "We are shifting the project timeline because the client changed their requirements" is vastly better than "We are shifting the project timeline." The missing "why" sends a Six into a spiral of worst-case speculation. Frame feedback as collaborative problem-solving: "I noticed an issue. Here is what I think. What do you see?" Sixes are excellent at finding risks and gaps. Inviting them into the analysis honors their strength.

**What to avoid:** Dismissing their concerns as negativity or anxiety. When a Six raises a potential problem, they are not being pessimistic. They are doing risk analysis, and it is usually accurate. The manager who says "Don't worry about it" to a Six is the manager who ends up dealing with exactly the problem the Six predicted. Also avoid sudden changes without explanation. Sixes can handle change. They cannot handle unexplained change.

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Increased questioning that feels like testing. When a Six starts asking more questions than usual, especially questions about decisions that were already made, they are losing trust. They are checking whether the foundation is still solid. The fix is not to answer impatiently. It is to rebuild the transparency. Share more context, not less. Explain your reasoning proactively. A Six who feels in the loop will stop testing.

### Type 7: The Enthusiast

**What motivates them:** Variety, possibility, and the chance to generate ideas. Sevens are the brainstormers, the connectors, the people who see opportunities where everyone else sees obstacles. They bring energy that lifts entire teams out of stagnation.

**How to give feedback:** With enthusiasm first, then structure. "Your ideas in the last brainstorm were strong. Three of them are worth pursuing. Let's pick one and take it all the way." The Seven will hear the affirmation and the challenge in equal measure. Frame accountability as an exciting mission, not a restriction: "I want to see what happens when you fully commit to this one thing."

**What to avoid:** Micromanaging their process. Sevens need room to move. But also avoid confusing their energy with follow-through. A Seven who says "I'm on it!" may genuinely be excited, and may also have three other things they said "I'm on it!" to this week. The management challenge with a Seven is channeling energy, not generating it. Build in check-ins that feel collaborative, not punitive. "Where are we with Project X?" works better than "Did you finish Project X?"

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Scattered attention and an increase in new ideas without completion of old ones. A healthy Seven generates ideas and finishes them. An unhealthy Seven generates ideas to escape the anxiety of finishing. If your Seven is on their fourth initiative this quarter and has completed none, they are not lazy. They are avoiding the discomfort that comes with completion, because finishing means being evaluated, and evaluation carries the risk of being found lacking.

### Type 8: The Challenger

**What motivates them:** Honesty, respect, and the opportunity to have real impact. Eights want to move mountains. Give them something that matters and the authority to execute, and they will deliver with a force that transforms teams.

**How to give feedback:** Directly. No softening, no preamble, no compliment sandwich. Eights can smell inauthenticity from across the room, and diplomatic packaging reads as weakness or manipulation. Say what you mean. "This part of the project is not where it needs to be. Here is what I need from you." An Eight will respect you for the directness and respond with action.

**What to avoid:** Going behind their back. If an Eight discovers that you discussed a concern about them with someone else before coming to them, the trust is damaged in a way that is very difficult to repair. Also avoid framing requests as suggestions when they are actually requirements. "It might be nice if..." reads as optional. "I need this by Thursday" reads as clear. Eights prefer clear.

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Increasing confrontation or control. An Eight under stress does not withdraw. They push harder. They challenge decisions more aggressively, take unilateral action, and start treating collaboration as inefficiency. This looks like a power grab, but it is usually a stress response: if I cannot trust the system, I will control the system. The intervention is a direct conversation: "I notice you are carrying more than your share. What is not working?" Ask it face-to-face. An Eight will not answer that question in an email.

### Type 9: The Peacemaker

**What motivates them:** Harmony, inclusion, and the feeling that their perspective matters. Nines are often the team's emotional thermostat. They sense tension before anyone names it, mediate conflicts nobody else notices, and create an environment where people feel safe.

**How to give feedback:** Gently but clearly, and then give them space to respond on their own timeline. Nines process slowly because they consider everyone's perspective before forming their own. Ask for their opinion and then wait. The pause before a Nine speaks is not hesitation. It is them locating an opinion that has been buried under everyone else's.

**What to avoid:** Mistaking their silence for agreement. When a Nine says nothing in a meeting, it almost never means they agree. It means they have decided that voicing their disagreement is not worth the conflict. If you need their real opinion, ask for it directly and make it safe to disagree: "I genuinely want to hear a different perspective. What would you push back on?" Also avoid putting them in high-conflict roles without support. Nines can handle conflict. They just need to know the relationship will survive it.

**Warning sign they are disengaging:** Passive compliance followed by sudden eruption. A Nine who agrees to everything without pushback is accumulating frustration. When it finally surfaces, weeks or months later, it looks disproportionate to the current situation. That is because it is not about the current situation. It is about the fifty small things they did not say. The manager who checks in regularly with "What is one thing you would change about how we work together?" can prevent the buildup before it reaches the eruption point.
