## Managing Each Type

### Type 1: The Reformer

Give clear expectations and acknowledge their effort, not just results. They need to know their standards are respected. When delivering feedback, lead with what they did well, then be specific about what to adjust. Vague criticism like "make it better" is a One's worst nightmare.

### Type 2: The Helper

Check in personally. Ask what *they* need. Recognize their contributions publicly. And watch for signs of overextension. When a Two starts saying "I'm fine" with a tight smile, they're not fine.

### Type 3: The Achiever

Set clear goals and get out of their way. Offer honest feedback privately, and frame it as strategy for improvement, not critique of performance. Remind them (gently, repeatedly) that failure is data, not identity.

### Type 4: The Individualist

Honor their creative process. Give meaningful work, not busywork. Validate their feelings without getting lost in them. When they withdraw after feedback, give them a beat, then follow up with genuine curiosity about their perspective.

### Type 5: The Investigator

Respect their need for preparation time. Send agendas in advance. Don't ambush them in meetings. When you need their input, give them the question in writing first and let them come back to you. The answer will be worth the wait.

### Type 6: The Loyalist

Be transparent about decisions and changes. Explain the "why" behind every significant shift. Don't dismiss their concerns as negativity; reframe them as the risk analysis your team needs. A Six who feels heard becomes your most loyal, thorough collaborator.

### Type 7: The Enthusiast

Channel their energy, don't suppress it. Give variety within structure. But hold them accountable for completion. A Seven who finishes something and gets recognized for the follow-through (not just the idea) will start to build that muscle.

### Type 8: The Challenger

Be direct. Don't sugarcoat. Show respect by being honest, and never go behind their back. If you disagree with an Eight, say so to their face. They'll respect you more for it than for any amount of diplomatic avoidance.

### Type 9: The Peacemaker

Ask for their opinion explicitly and then wait. The pause before a Nine speaks is not hesitation; it's them locating an opinion that's been buried under everyone else's. Create safe space for disagreement. Don't mistake their silence for agreement. It almost never is.
