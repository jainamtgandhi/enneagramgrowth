Reading about the Enneagram is useful. Practicing it with the people you work with is transformative. These five exercises take the theory off the page and into the room where it matters, not as personality trivia, but as a shared language for how your team actually functions.

> **These exercises work best when the team has shared context.** Consider reading [How Each Type Shows Up at Work](/workplace/type-styles) together first.

## Practice This

**The Type-Sharing Exercise.** In a team meeting, each person shares their type (or suspected type) and one thing they want others to know about how they work best. Keep it to two minutes per person. No debates about typing. No "I think you're actually a..." responses. The goal is understanding, not accuracy. A simple prompt: "When I'm at my best at work, I need ___. When I'm stressed, I tend to ___." Even people who don't know their type can answer those questions, and the answers will teach the team more than any personality report.

**The Center Check-In.** Before a big decision, pause and ask: "What does the Body Center say? What does the Heart Center say? What does the Head Center say?" In practice, this sounds like: "What does our gut tell us? (Body) How will this affect people and relationships? (Heart) What are the risks and unknowns we haven't addressed? (Head)" Even if your team doesn't have all three centers represented, this framework ensures you're considering action, feeling, *and* analysis. Write the three answers on a whiteboard. The missing perspective is almost always the one that would have prevented the next problem.

**The Conflict Protocol.** When tension arises between two people, each person answers three questions privately in writing before any discussion: "What am I feeling right now?" "What do I actually need?" "What story am I telling myself about the other person?" Then share the answers. Most conflicts dissolve, or at least transform, when people hear each other's honest answers. The story question is especially powerful: "I was telling myself you didn't respect my expertise" hits very differently from "You never listen to me."

**The Burnout Radar.** Once a month, each team member privately rates their energy on a 1-10 scale and shares one thing that would help. No justification needed. No performance-review consequences. This normalizes talking about stress before it becomes a crisis. Track the numbers over time. A steady decline from 7 to 5 to 3 is a signal that something structural needs to change, not just a person who needs a vacation.

**The Missing Center Audit.** Once a quarter, map your team by center. You don't need exact types; just note whether each person tends to lead with Body (action, instinct), Heart (relationships, image), or Head (analysis, strategy). Look at the balance. If a center is underrepresented, designate someone (rotating) to "hold" that perspective in key meetings. A Head-heavy team appoints a Heart advocate who asks: "How will this affect morale? Who haven't we consulted?" A Heart-heavy team appoints a Body advocate who asks: "What's the first concrete step? When do we start?"
