*Common for Types 1 and 8*

**What's happening.** Picture a woman named Mara standing in her kitchen at 10 p.m. on a Thursday. She just spent forty minutes reorganizing the spice rack, not because it needed it, but because her teenage son left his dishes in the sink and something inside her snapped. Not the dishes themselves; the *disorder* of it. She's now rewriting tomorrow's schedule on a fresh notepad because the old one had a crossed-out entry. She knows this is not rational. She can feel the exhaustion in her body. But stopping feels like letting the world tilt sideways.

For a One like Mara, the controlling looks meticulous and principled. For an Eight, it looks more like dominance. Picture a man named Jared who discovers his team submitted a proposal without his final review. His first reaction isn't "Let me see how it turned out." It's a flash of heat in his chest and an urgent need to call someone and establish that this will not happen again. The proposal might be excellent. That's not the point. The point is that something happened outside his control, and his nervous system registered that as danger.

### Why it happens

For Ones, control is a response to an inner critic that says the world should be better and it's your job to fix it. The critic never rests, so neither do you. For Eights, control is armor against vulnerability; if you're in charge, no one can hurt you. Both types learned early that if you don't take charge, things fall apart, and the consequences fall on you.

### What to do right now

Put your hands flat on a surface. A table, a desk, your own thighs. Feel the pressure against your palms. Say out loud: "I can let this be imperfect." Take three slow breaths, inhaling for four counts and exhaling for six. If you're about to correct someone, wait ten minutes. Set a literal timer on your phone. If the urge is still there after ten minutes, ask yourself: "Is this about quality, or about my discomfort?" Then ask a harder question: "What am I afraid will happen if I let this go?"

### The longer practice

Each day for one week, deliberately leave one thing "wrong." A crooked picture frame. An email with a slightly awkward sentence that you'd normally rewrite three times. A dish left in the sink overnight. Sit with the discomfort. Write one sentence about what you notice: the itchy restlessness, the voice in your head that says you're being lazy, the catastrophic prediction that doesn't come true. Notice that the world doesn't collapse. Over time, this builds tolerance for imperfection and loosens the grip of control. After the first week, extend it. One imperfect thing per day becomes a practice that slowly teaches your nervous system that imperfection is not emergency.

For Eights specifically: practice delegating one task per week to someone who will do it differently than you would. Don't check on it. Don't "just make sure." Let the outcome be what it is. Notice whether your fear (that it will be done badly, that you'll be exposed) actually comes true. Usually, it doesn't.

### When to seek help

If the need to control is causing conflict in your relationships, if you can't sleep because things feel "undone," if anger at imperfection is constant and escalating, or if your body is holding the tension in ways that are affecting your health (headaches, jaw pain, stomach issues), a therapist who understands perfectionism or control patterns can help you find the need underneath the need for control.
