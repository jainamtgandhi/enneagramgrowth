*Common for Types 2 and 9*

**What's happening.** Picture a man named Henry who just worked a ten-hour day, cooked dinner for his partner and two kids, helped his neighbor move a couch, and agreed to chair the school fundraiser committee, all in the same Tuesday. He's lying in bed at midnight, exhausted, mentally drafting the fundraiser email, when his partner rolls over and says, "You seem tense." Henry says, "I'm fine." He is not fine. He's furious. But the fury confuses him because he doesn't know who he's angry at. Everyone only asked. He's the one who said yes.

For a Two like Henry, the giving is strategic at its root, even though it doesn't feel that way. He gives because on some level, he learned that being needed is the only reliable path to being loved. If he stops being useful, the unspoken terror is that people will stop needing him, and then they'll leave. The giving isn't generous in the way it appears. It's a contract the other person never signed.

For a Nine, the giving looks different. Picture a woman named Elise who hasn't chosen a restaurant in six years. She always defers. "Whatever you want." "I'm easy." "I don't have a preference." She does have a preference. She wants Thai food. But the moment someone else suggests Italian, her preference dissolves like it was never there, and she merges with their choice because the tiny friction of disagreement feels more costly than the quiet loss of getting what she wants.

### Why it happens

Twos give to earn love. Nines merge to avoid conflict. Both types learned that their own needs were less important, or even dangerous to express. For Twos, the lesson was usually explicit: "Good people take care of others." For Nines, it was more atmospheric: the family system worked better when they didn't take up space, so they learned to shrink.

### What to do right now

Before your next "yes," pause. Put your hand on your chest and ask: "Do I actually want to do this, or am I afraid of what happens if I say no?" Practice this sentence: "Let me think about it and get back to you." You don't owe anyone an immediate answer. If you're a Two, notice the panic that arises when you imagine saying no. That panic is not a signal that you should say yes. It's a signal that your sense of worth is tangled up with your usefulness. If you're a Nine, notice the fog that descends when someone asks what you want. That fog is not the absence of desire. It's a habit of suppression so practiced it feels like emptiness.

### The longer practice

Every morning for one month, write down one thing *you* want today. Not for anyone else. Not to help or accommodate. Something just for you. It can be tiny: a walk alone, a meal you choose, an hour of silence. Do it without explaining or justifying. Don't tell anyone why. If you're a Two, resist the urge to make your self-care into another form of service ("I need to rest so I can be better for others"). No. You need to rest because you are a human being and rest is not something you earn. If you're a Nine, the hardest part will be the first step: actually knowing what you want. If "I don't know" comes up, stay with it. Ask again, softer: "If I *did* know, what might it be?"

Weekly check-in: At the end of each week, count how many times you said yes when you meant no. Don't judge the number. Just notice it. Awareness is the practice. Over weeks, the number will start to shift on its own.

### When to seek help

If you've lost your sense of self entirely, if you feel invisible even in close relationships, if saying no triggers panic or if you physically cannot identify your own desires, a therapist can help you reconnect with your own needs and boundaries. This is particularly important for Nines, whose self-forgetting can run so deep that it resembles clinical dissociation.
