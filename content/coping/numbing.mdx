*Common for Type 9*

**What's happening.** Picture a man named Leon at a family dinner. His mother asks what he wants to do for his birthday. His sister suggests a restaurant. His brother suggests a hiking trip. His partner suggests a quiet dinner at home. Leon smiles and says, "Any of those sound great." He means it. And he doesn't mean it. What he means is: he cannot locate his own preference. It's like reaching for something in a dark room; his hand closes on air. He knows he should have an opinion. He's forty-two years old. But the machinery that generates preferences has been offline for so long that "I don't mind" has become a reflex so total it passes for personality.

Later, Leon is on the couch. Someone asks what he's watching. He looks at the screen and realizes he doesn't know. He's been scrolling through options for twenty minutes without selecting anything, and now he's staring at a show he chose by accident. He's physically present but emotionally somewhere else entirely. This is the fog, and for Nines, it's as familiar as breathing.

### Why it happens

Nines learned that their presence, their anger, their desires were too much or too inconvenient. Perhaps they grew up in a loud family where the quiet child was the "easy" child, and being easy meant being safe. Perhaps they learned that having wants creates conflict, and conflict creates loss. Numbing out is a survival strategy: if you don't want anything, you can't be disappointed. If you don't feel, you can't be hurt. The problem is that you also can't be fully alive. The volume knob that turned down the pain also turned down the joy, the desire, the sense of being a person with edges and preferences and a right to take up space.

### What to do right now

Engage one sense intensely. Bite into something sour, a lemon wedge, a sour candy, anything that jolts. Splash cold water on your face. Smell something strong: coffee grounds, peppermint oil, a cut orange. The goal is to interrupt the fog and bring you back into your body, where your feelings actually live. The numbness is an exit from embodiment, and the senses are the re-entry point.

Then try this: complete the sentence "Right now, I want ___" out loud. Not "I should" or "It would be nice if." "I want." If nothing comes, that's okay. Say "I want to know what I want." That counts. The wanting doesn't have to be big. "I want a glass of water" is a victory if you've spent the last hour merged with someone else's desires.

### The longer practice

Three times a day, stop and ask yourself: "What am I feeling right now?" Set an alarm if you need to. If the answer is "nothing" or "fine," dig one layer deeper. "Fine" often means "I haven't checked." "Nothing" often means "something, but I turned it off before I could identify it." Keep a feelings journal. Even one word per entry builds the muscle of self-awareness. Over weeks, the one-word entries might expand to sentences. Over months, you might start noticing feelings *in the moment* instead of retroactively. That's real progress.

Weekly: make one decision per week that is *yours alone*. Not a compromise. Not a deferral. Pick the restaurant. Pick the movie. Pick the paint color. Tell the people in your life, "I chose this." When someone suggests an alternative, practice saying, "I appreciate that, but I'd like to try my choice this time." Notice what happens in your body when you assert a preference. The discomfort, the guilt, the fear of being seen as selfish: these are the guardians at the gate of your own aliveness. They don't have to leave. You just have to walk past them.

Monthly: write a list of ten things you care about. Not things you should care about. Things that actually stir something in you. If you can only come up with three, that's a start. Put the list somewhere you'll see it daily. It's easy for Nines to lose themselves in other people's priorities; the list is an anchor to your own.

### When to seek help

If the numbness is persistent, if you feel disconnected from your own life, if dissociation is affecting your ability to function or maintain relationships, or if you realize you've been "going along with" your entire life without ever choosing it, a therapist can help you come back to yourself safely. For Nines, the therapeutic process itself can be a revelation, because it's an hour a week where someone is specifically interested in *your* inner experience. The simple act of being asked "What do you feel?" by someone who waits for the real answer can be the beginning of a profound reconnection.
