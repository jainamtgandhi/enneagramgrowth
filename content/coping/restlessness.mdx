*Common for Type 7*

**What's happening.** Picture a man named Owen on vacation. He's on a beach. It's beautiful. His wife is reading next to him. The ocean is right there. And Owen is on his phone, looking up restaurants for dinner, checking if there's a snorkeling tour tomorrow, and mentally redesigning his home office. He's been sitting for twelve minutes. It feels like an hour. His wife says, "Can you just be here?" and Owen says, "I am here," while simultaneously googling "best day trips from Cancun."

Owen isn't rude. He isn't distracted in the clinical sense. He's running from something, and the running is so habitual that it feels like enthusiasm. The restlessness says: "There's something better somewhere else." But if you listen closely, what it really says is: "If I stop, something uncomfortable will catch up to me."

### Why it happens

Sevens learned that pain is to be avoided and pleasure is safety. Stillness creates space for uncomfortable feelings to surface: grief, loss, limitation, the heavy stuff that busy-ness keeps at arm's length. The mind scrambles to fill that space with plans, ideas, and stimulation because, on a deep level, the Seven believes that facing the dark feelings would be annihilating. It wouldn't be. But the avoidance is so total that the Seven has never stayed long enough to find out.

### What to do right now

Sit down. Set a timer for three minutes. Do nothing. No phone. No music. No planning. When your mind generates an idea, a plan, an escape, notice it and label it: "That's my mind looking for an exit." You don't have to follow every impulse. Just notice it. See how many exit attempts your mind generates in three minutes. Don't judge the number; just count. This is data about how hard your inner system works to keep you from sitting still.

If three minutes feels impossible, start with one. One minute of deliberate stillness, with attention on the breath, is a radical act for a Seven. The discomfort you feel is not evidence that something is wrong. It's evidence that you're touching the edge of what you usually avoid.

### The longer practice

Develop a regular stillness practice. Even five minutes of sitting with no input. No music, no podcast, no planning, no background television. Let the discomfort come. Let it sit next to you like an unfamiliar guest. The feelings underneath the restlessness are not dangerous. They're just unfamiliar. Over time, they become less unfamiliar, and the need to outrun them weakens.

Weekly: instead of adding a new activity to your week, subtract one. Cancel a plan. Leave an evening empty. When the emptiness produces anxiety, stay with it. Write down what you're feeling. For many Sevens, this is the first time they've met their own sadness, frustration, or grief without a door open behind them. It won't be pleasant. It will be important.

Monthly: revisit one thing you abandoned. A project, a relationship, a creative pursuit you started with excitement and dropped when the novelty wore off. You don't have to finish it. Just sit with it. Ask yourself: "What was I avoiding when I left this?" Sometimes the answer is practical (the project genuinely wasn't worth completing). Sometimes it's revealing (you left because it got hard, and hard felt like a cage).

### When to seek help

If you're using substances, spending, or constant activity to avoid feelings, if you're unable to be present with people you love, if the restlessness is affecting your ability to function, or if you've realized that you've been running for so long that you don't know what you're running from anymore, a professional can help you learn to be with yourself. The specific skill a therapist can offer Sevens is the experience of sitting with discomfort in the presence of another person who isn't trying to fix it or escape it. That experience, replicated enough times, rewires the assumption that pain must be avoided at all costs.
