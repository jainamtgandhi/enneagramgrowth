*Common for Type 4*

**What's happening.** Picture a woman named Camille on a Sunday afternoon. The light is beautiful. Her apartment is warm. Nothing is wrong. And yet there's a weight on her chest that she can't explain, a gravitational pull toward sadness that seems to exist independent of circumstances. Something beautiful makes her ache instead of happy. She plays a song that brings tears, and the tears feel more real than the pleasant afternoon did. She knows she's choosing the melancholy. She knows. But the alternative, ordinary contentment, feels thin and false, like wearing someone else's clothes.

The world feels gray. Not always. But often enough that Camille has started to wonder if the grayness is the truth and the good moments are the illusion. She's drawn toward the melancholy like a magnet, and the part that scares her is that she doesn't entirely want it to stop. The sadness feels like *her*. Losing it would feel like losing herself.

### Why it happens

Fours internalized a belief that something fundamental is missing. Other people seem to have an ease of belonging, a natural contentment, that the Four can observe but never access. The sadness feels more real, more authentic, than happiness. Letting go of it feels like losing your identity. This is the Four's central paradox: the wound became the identity, and healing the wound feels like self-erasure.

### What to do right now

Move your body. Not to "fix" the sadness, but to shift its grip. Walk outside. Feel wind on your skin. Name three things you can see, three things you can hear, and three things you can touch. This is not toxic positivity; it's sensory grounding. You're not betraying your depth by grounding yourself in the present. You're adding another channel of experience alongside the emotional one. The sadness doesn't have to leave for you to also feel the wind.

A specific practice: when the melancholy pulls you inward, do something with your hands. Wash dishes. Knead bread. Arrange objects on a shelf. Fours live so deeply in emotional interiority that the body becomes forgotten territory. Physical action reconnects you to the present without requiring you to "cheer up," which is advice Fours rightly despise.

### The longer practice

Start a gratitude practice, but make it a Four's gratitude practice: instead of listing "good" things, write about beauty you encountered today. A shadow on a wall. The way someone laughed. A color you noticed for the first time. Let yourself notice that beauty exists alongside the ache, not replacing it, but coexisting with it. This is not about choosing happiness over sadness. It's about expanding the emotional palette so that melancholy isn't the only color in the room.

Weekly: choose one ordinary activity and bring your full creative attention to it. Cooking. Walking a familiar route. Sitting in a specific chair. The Four's longing is for something extraordinary, and that longing makes ordinary life feel inadequate. But when you bring deep attention to something ordinary, it becomes extraordinary. That's not a platitude. It's a practice, and it rewires the assumption that depth requires suffering.

### When to seek help

If the sadness is persistent and debilitating, if you're isolating for days at a time, if you've lost interest in the creative work that normally sustains you, or if you're having thoughts of self-harm, please reach out to a mental health professional. Depression and the Four's constitutional melancholy can look similar from the outside, but they require different responses. A Four's melancholy is a temperamental baseline that can coexist with a full life. Depression is a clinical condition that responds to treatment. You deserve to know which one you're dealing with, and a professional can help you tell the difference.
