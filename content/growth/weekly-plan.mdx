## Practice This: A Week-Long Plan

This is not a program to complete. It is a template to repeat. Each day focuses on a different dimension of growth, giving you a balanced rhythm that touches body, heart, and head throughout the week.

You will need: a notebook or notes app, and the willingness to be honest with yourself for ten minutes a day. That is it. No special equipment. No perfect conditions. Start the week you read this.

### Monday: Awareness Baseline

Set three phone alarms at random points throughout the day. Not at mealtimes or transitions, because you are already somewhat conscious at those moments. Pick times when you are likely to be deep in autopilot: mid-morning, mid-afternoon, and early evening.

When each alarm goes off, pause whatever you are doing. Do not finish the sentence you are typing. Do not wait for a convenient moment. Pause now, and ask yourself two questions:

1. *Am I on autopilot right now? What pattern is running?*
2. *What was I feeling one second before this alarm went off?*

Write down what you notice in one sentence. Do not judge it. Do not fix it. Just record it.

By the end of Monday you will have three snapshots of your unconscious operating system. Most people are surprised by what they find. Not dramatic revelations, usually. Small things: the low-grade irritation they did not realize was there. The restlessness they had been powering through. The conversation they were mentally rehearsing instead of being present. These snapshots become your baseline. You will compare them to later weeks and notice the shifts.

### Tuesday: Body Day

Your body knows things your mind has not caught up to yet. Today, you practice listening to it.

Start the morning with the somatic check-in from [Practices by Center](/growth/by-center): sit quietly for three minutes and scan from head to feet, noticing where you carry tension. Do not try to release it. Just map it. Jaw, shoulders, chest, belly, hands. Where is the holding?

At one meal today (choose whichever is most often rushed), eat without screens. No phone, no laptop, no podcast. Just the food. Notice the first bite: temperature, texture, flavor. Eat slowly enough that you can detect when your body signals satisfaction before your plate is empty. Most people discover they usually eat past that signal because they are distracted.

Before bed, write down where you carried tension today. Was it the same places as this morning, or had it migrated? Did anything shift it? The goal is not relaxation. It is data. You are learning the language your body speaks, which is the language your type's pattern most wants you to ignore.

### Wednesday: Heart Day

Today is about connection, specifically the kind you do not get credit for.

Sometime during the day, do one act of kindness that nobody will know about. Leave a generous tip on a small order. Send an encouraging message to someone you have not talked to in months. Clean something in a shared space without mentioning it. The act itself matters less than the condition: no one can thank you, praise you, or know it was you.

This exercise hits each type differently. For Twos, the challenge is the anonymity: kindness without recognition feels incomplete. For Threes, the challenge is the invisibility: effort without credit feels pointless. For Fives, the challenge is the engagement: reaching out at all costs energy. For everyone, it creates a data point: *What is my relationship with kindness when no audience is watching?*

Before bed, sit for five minutes and ask: *Who was I today when no one was watching?* Write whatever comes. Do not edit.

### Thursday: Head Day

Today, you practice doing one thing at a time.

Pick one hour, any hour, and commit to single-tasking. If you are writing, you are only writing. If you are in a meeting, you are only in a meeting. If you are washing dishes, you are only washing dishes. No checking your phone between tasks. No toggling between tabs. No mentally rehearsing the next thing while doing the current thing.

This is harder than it sounds. The mind's default mode is to split attention, not because multitasking is efficient (research consistently shows it is not), but because focused attention on a single thing creates a kind of stillness that feels uncomfortable. Your type will resist this stillness in its own characteristic way. Sevens will feel bored. Threes will feel unproductive. Sixes will feel anxious about what they are missing. Ones will feel like they should be doing something more important. Notice which resistance shows up. That is useful information.

Before bed, sit in stillness for five minutes. Not meditation, exactly. Just stillness. No task, no goal, no prompt. Let your thoughts come and go without following them. If this feels impossibly difficult, start with three minutes. The point is not duration. It is the practice of being present without producing anything.

### Friday: Type-Specific Day

Today is your day. Do the practice listed for your type in [Specific Practices by Type](/growth/by-type). If you do not know your type with certainty, pick the one that makes you most uncomfortable. That is probably the right one.

Commit at least thirty minutes to the practice. Not thirty minutes of thinking about the practice. Thirty minutes of doing it. If you are a One, spend thirty minutes being deliberately imperfect. If you are a Seven, sit with discomfort for thirty minutes. If you are a Nine, state preferences for thirty minutes. Longer is fine. Shorter defeats the purpose, because the real growth happens after the initial resistance fades, and that usually takes fifteen to twenty minutes.

Journal for ten minutes afterward. Three prompts:

1. *What did I feel in my body during the practice?*
2. *What story did my mind tell me about why I should stop?*
3. *What surprised me?*

The third question is the most important. Growth almost always contains a surprise: a moment where reality diverges from what your pattern predicted. Those surprises are the cracks in the autopilot, the moments where a new response becomes possible.

### Saturday: Relationship Day

Today, you practice being a different version of yourself in one conversation.

Choose one interaction, a conversation with a partner, a friend, a family member, a colleague, and consciously do the opposite of your type's default communication style:

- **If you usually give advice,** just listen. Let someone struggle without rescuing them.
- **If you usually withdraw,** stay engaged. Keep the conversation going past the point where you would normally retreat.
- **If you usually take charge,** let someone else lead. Follow their agenda, their pace, their direction.
- **If you usually deflect with humor,** be earnest. Say something sincere without a joke attached.
- **If you usually accommodate,** disagree about something. Gently, but clearly.
- **If you usually analyze,** express a feeling. "I felt sad about that" instead of "I think the issue is..."

Notice what happens in the other person. Notice what happens in you. The discomfort is the growth. The other person's response is the data.

Write down one thing you learned from the conversation. Not about the other person. About yourself.

### Sunday: Rest and Review

No exercises today. No practices. No growth edges. Just rest and reflection.

Reread your notes from the week. All of them: the Monday snapshots, the body data, the heart journal, the stillness observations, the type-specific surprises, the relationship experiment. Read them as though they were written by someone else. What patterns do you notice?

- Where did you resist most? (That is probably your deepest growth edge.)
- Where did you feel a genuine shift? (That is where your next breakthrough lives.)
- What surprised you? (That is the new information your autopilot did not expect.)

Write a letter to yourself about what you learned this week. Keep it short (half a page is plenty). Do not make it a performance. Just be honest. Keep the letter. Read it again before you start the cycle next week.

## Repeating the Cycle

This plan is designed to repeat. The first week teaches you the format. The second week deepens the observations. By the third or fourth week, you will start to notice something that only repetition reveals: the shifts are cumulative. Your Monday snapshots will change. Your body awareness will sharpen. The type-specific practice will feel different each time because *you* are different each time.

Some weeks will feel transformative. Others will feel flat. Both are part of the process. Growth is not a highlight reel. It is a rhythm.

A few notes on sustaining the practice:

- **Skip days, not weeks.** If you miss a Tuesday, do not scrap the whole week. Just pick up on Wednesday.
- **Rotate the type-specific practice.** After a month, try a different type's practice on Fridays, one that targets a secondary growth edge (your wing or arrow type). See what happens.
- **Share the plan.** If you have a partner, friend, or colleague also interested in the Enneagram, doing this week in parallel and comparing notes deepens both your practices enormously.
