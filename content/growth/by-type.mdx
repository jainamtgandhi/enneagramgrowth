## Specific Practices by Type

Beyond the [center-based practices](/growth/by-center), each type has particular growth edges that benefit from targeted attention. These exercises are designed to gently push against the specific pattern that keeps each type stuck.

### Type 1: The Reformer

Practice doing something badly on purpose. Cook a meal without a recipe. Draw something without trying to make it good. Send an email without rereading it. The inner critic will scream. Let it. This builds tolerance for imperfection, which is the One's deepest growth edge.

### Type 2: The Helper

Practice receiving without reciprocating. When someone gives you a compliment, say "thank you" and stop. When someone does something kind for you, do not immediately figure out how to return the favor. Let yourself be given to. Notice how uncomfortable it is. That discomfort is your growth edge.

### Type 3: The Achiever

Practice stopping before you are done. Leave a task at 85% complete and walk away. Sit in the discomfort of "not finished." Notice the voice that says you are falling behind. Ask it: "Behind what? Compared to whom?" The Three's growth edge is learning that they are enough even when they are not achieving.

### Type 4: The Individualist

Practice ordinary happiness. When something good and simple happens, resist the urge to add complexity or depth to it. A sunny walk is just a sunny walk. A nice cup of coffee is just a nice cup of coffee. You do not need to find the melancholy underneath the beauty every time. Let simple things be simple. That is your growth edge.

### Type 5: The Investigator

Practice sharing before you are ready. Offer an opinion before you have finished researching. Tell someone what you are feeling before you have fully processed it. Say "I don't know yet, but here's what I think so far." The Five's growth edge is learning that incomplete expression is still valuable.

### Type 6: The Loyalist

Practice trusting your own judgment. The next time you face a decision, make it without consulting anyone. Do not poll friends. Do not google it. Decide, and then live with the decision for a full day before second-guessing it. Notice how your body feels during that day. The Six's growth edge is learning that their own inner authority is reliable.

### Type 7: The Enthusiast

Practice sitting with pain. When something hurts (a rejection, a disappointment, a loss), resist the urge to reframe it, plan something fun to compensate, or distract yourself. Sit with it for ten minutes. Let it be uncomfortable. Notice that it does not destroy you. The Seven's growth edge is learning that pain is survivable, which, ironically, makes joy more accessible.

### Type 8: The Challenger

Practice vulnerability with someone safe. Tell one person something you are afraid of. Not the big, noble fears (injustice, betrayal). The small, tender ones. "I am afraid you will leave." "I am afraid I am not as strong as I seem." "I feel lonely." Say it out loud. The Eight's growth edge is learning that softness is not weakness.

### Type 9: The Peacemaker

Practice stating a preference when it does not matter. "I would rather eat Thai food tonight." "I would prefer the window seat." "I do not want to watch that show." Start with low-stakes preferences and build from there. The Nine's growth edge is learning that having a voice does not create the conflict they fear.
