## The Enneagram at Work

You spend a staggering amount of your life at work. And most of the friction you experience there isn't about strategy, deadlines, or even competence. It's about people. The colleague who rewrites your email before sending it. The manager who needs to process every decision out loud. The teammate who disappears for two hours and comes back with a brilliant solution no one asked for.

Here's what makes workplace conflict so stubborn: almost none of it is about what people think it's about. The argument over the project timeline isn't really about the timeline. It's about a One who can't ship something that isn't excellent, a Three who needs to hit the quarterly number, and a Six who is terrified that rushing will cause a disaster nobody's anticipated. Three people, three invisible motivational systems, all grinding against each other while everyone pretends the disagreement is about a Gantt chart.

The Enneagram doesn't put people in boxes at work. It opens a window into *why* your coworkers do what they do, and why you react the way you react. When you understand that your teammate's relentless follow-up emails aren't micromanaging but a Six's way of feeling safe, or that your boss's bluntness isn't aggression but an Eight's version of respect, everything shifts.

This isn't about labeling people over coffee. It's about building the kind of awareness that turns a dysfunctional team into one that actually leverages its differences. The most effective teams aren't the ones where everyone thinks alike. They're the ones where people understand *how* each person thinks, and make room for it.

## How Each Type Shows Up at Work

**Type 1: The Reformer.** Picture a project manager named Grace who stays an hour late to reformat a slide deck that was "fine." Her work is thorough and principled, and her team trusts her quality instinctively. When she raises a concern, people listen, because she's usually right. But Grace's strength has a shadow. She sent a colleague back to revise a report four times on a Tuesday, each time pointing out issues that mattered to her but not to the client. Her team respects her standards but dreads her feedback, because "good enough" is a phrase that causes Grace physical discomfort. If you work with a One, know this: their corrections come from love of the work, not contempt for you. But they genuinely may not see the difference from the receiving end.

**Type 2: The Helper.** Meet Andre, who remembers every team member's birthday, knows who's going through a divorce, and always has snacks in his desk drawer. He's the relational glue that holds his department together. When a new hire is struggling, Andre is the one who checks in. But Andre also said yes to organizing the offsite, mentoring two interns, and covering a colleague's presentation, all in the same week. By Friday, he's running on fumes and quietly seething that nobody asked how *he's* doing. His blind spot is real: Andre advocates fiercely for everyone except himself, and his resentment leaks out in passive comments that confuse teammates who thought he was happy to help.

**Type 3: The Achiever.** Consider a sales director named Wei who hit 140% of quota last quarter and is already strategizing for next quarter before the celebration email goes out. Wei is the engine of her team. She sets goals, hits them, and makes it look effortless. People rally around her vision because her energy is genuinely infectious. But in a team retrospective last month, two quieter colleagues mentioned they felt steamrolled. Wei had cut off their input in a brainstorm because their ideas "weren't going to move the needle." She wasn't being cruel. She was being efficient. But efficiency without inclusion is just speed, and Wei is learning (slowly) that the ideas she dismissed might have been the ones that mattered most.

**Type 4: The Individualist.** Picture a designer named Soren who produces work with a signature quality his clients can't quite articulate but always notice. He sees what's missing in a way that elevates every project he touches. His creative instincts are the reason his team wins pitches. But Soren took a routine round of design feedback last week and went silent for two days. The feedback was constructive and specific. It didn't matter. To Soren, feedback on his work feels indistinguishable from feedback on his soul. His withdrawal wasn't dramatic; he just stopped contributing in meetings and responded to Slack messages with one-word answers. If you work with a Four, know that they need you to acknowledge what's working before you discuss what isn't. Not as flattery. As oxygen.

**Type 5: The Investigator.** Meet a data analyst named Yumi who actually reads the entire report. Every footnote. Every appendix. Her presentations are airtight because she has anticipated every question before it's asked. She brings clarity and calm analysis when everyone else is reacting emotionally. But Yumi guards her time like a scarce resource. She declined three meeting invitations last week, not because she was busy, but because she calculated that the meetings wouldn't yield enough value to justify the energy cost. She shares conclusions without showing her work, which makes her seem secretive when she's actually just efficient. If you ambush Yumi with a question in a hallway, you'll get a blank stare and a request to "send that in an email." It's not rudeness. It's a Five's need to prepare before they give you their thinking.

**Type 6: The Loyalist.** Picture a compliance manager named Darnell who spotted the contract vulnerability that saved his company from a lawsuit. His team calls him "the canary" because he senses danger before anyone else does. His loyalty and follow-through make him the backbone of any team he joins. But Darnell also delayed a product launch by two weeks because he wanted to run one more scenario analysis. His manager was frustrated. The product was ready. But Darnell couldn't sign off until he was sure, and "sure" is a destination he never quite reaches. His need for reassurance can exhaust leaders who mistake it for lack of confidence, when it's actually an excess of responsibility.

**Type 7: The Enthusiast.** Consider a brand strategist named Lucia who generates more ideas in a single brainstorm than most people produce in a month. She's the one who sees connections between markets nobody else noticed. Her energy pulls teams out of ruts and into possibility. But Lucia has started four strategic initiatives this quarter and finished one. When the work moves from exciting to tedious (which it always does, eventually), her attention migrates to the next shiny thing. She reframes problems so quickly that her team sometimes realizes, mid-pivot, that they never actually solved the original one. If you manage a Seven, your job is to channel the energy, not suppress it. But you also need to hold the line on completion.

**Type 8: The Challenger.** Meet a VP of operations named Marcus who said in a team meeting, "I don't care about your feelings about the restructuring. I care about whether it works." People know where they stand with Marcus. He's the one willing to have the conversation everyone else has been avoiding for months. His directness cuts through politics and gets things moving. But Marcus also spoke over three colleagues in that same meeting, dismissed a junior team member's concern as "not strategic enough," and didn't notice the silence he left in his wake. His blind spot isn't cruelty; it's a genuine inability to see that his volume is turning people off before they can hear his message.

**Type 9: The Peacemaker.** Picture a team lead named Hana who was asked by her director, "What do you think of the new org structure?" Hana paused, smiled, and said, "I think it could work." She had seventeen thoughts about the org structure, most of them critical. She shared none of them. Hana is the natural mediator, the one who sees every perspective and creates space for everyone to be heard. But she creates that space partly by vacating her own. She goes along with decisions she disagrees with, avoids conflict until it's too late to address gracefully, and struggles to assert her own priorities until they've been overridden so many times that she erupts, seemingly out of nowhere, and everyone is confused.

## Team Dynamics by Center

**Body Center teams (8, 9, 1)** are action-oriented. They want to *do* something. Decisions get made quickly, and there's a bias toward movement. Picture a leadership team of an Eight CEO, a One COO, and a Nine head of HR. In practice, the Eight drives decisions at speed, the One immediately starts refining and correcting the execution plan, and the Nine quietly absorbs the tension between them without voicing the objection that could prevent a major misstep. The shadow side: unacknowledged anger simmers beneath the surface. The Eight is openly frustrated, the One is silently resentful, and the Nine is numb. If your team skews Body, build in reflection pauses before big decisions. And create explicit space for the Nines to disagree before they go along with something they'll regret.

**Heart Center teams (2, 3, 4)** are relationally attuned. They care about how people feel, how things look, and whether the team is connected. Imagine a marketing department staffed mostly by Twos, Threes, and Fours. The Twos are making sure everyone feels supported. The Threes are driving toward the campaign deadline. The Fours are advocating for creative integrity. Sounds balanced until the first round of negative feedback arrives. The Twos feel unappreciated ("We did all this work and nobody cares"). The Threes feel exposed ("This makes us look incompetent"). The Fours feel unseen ("Nobody understands what we were going for"). Suddenly the room is thick with unspoken shame, and nobody is addressing the actual feedback. If your team skews Heart, create explicit permission for direct feedback and make it safe to separate work quality from personal worth.

**Head Center teams (5, 6, 7)** are analytical and strategic. They think before they act, anticipate problems, and generate ideas. Now picture a product team with a Five lead engineer, a Six product manager, and a Seven design lead. The Five wants more data before committing to a direction. The Six wants to map out every risk scenario. The Seven wants to pivot to a more exciting approach. A month passes. The whiteboard is covered in frameworks. The Slack channel is full of "what ifs." Nothing has shipped. The shadow side: analysis paralysis. They can overthink, over-plan, and delay action while chasing certainty that never comes. If your team skews Head, set deadlines for decisions, reward action (not just analysis), and appoint someone (ideally a Body type) to call the question when the thinking has gone on long enough.

**The missing-center problem.** The most common team dysfunction isn't conflict between types. It's the absence of an entire center. A startup founded by two Fives and a Seven (all Head types) will generate brilliant strategy and zero emotional awareness. They'll lose talent because nobody feels valued. A nonprofit run by Twos and Fours (all Heart types) will have deep mission alignment and terrible execution. They'll plan a fundraiser with beautiful invitations and no budget. When you notice a blind spot in your team, look for the missing center. You don't need to hire for it (though that helps). You just need to consciously include it in your process.

## Managing Each Type

- **Type 1:** Give clear expectations and acknowledge their effort, not just results. They need to know their standards are respected. When delivering feedback, lead with what they did well, then be specific about what to adjust. Vague criticism like "make it better" is a One's worst nightmare.
- **Type 2:** Check in personally. Ask what *they* need. Recognize their contributions publicly. And watch for signs of overextension. When a Two starts saying "I'm fine" with a tight smile, they're not fine.
- **Type 3:** Set clear goals and get out of their way. Offer honest feedback privately, and frame it as strategy for improvement, not critique of performance. Remind them (gently, repeatedly) that failure is data, not identity.
- **Type 4:** Honor their creative process. Give meaningful work, not busywork. Validate their feelings without getting lost in them. When they withdraw after feedback, give them a beat, then follow up with genuine curiosity about their perspective.
- **Type 5:** Respect their need for preparation time. Send agendas in advance. Don't ambush them in meetings. When you need their input, give them the question in writing first and let them come back to you. The answer will be worth the wait.
- **Type 6:** Be transparent about decisions and changes. Explain the "why" behind every significant shift. Don't dismiss their concerns as negativity; reframe them as the risk analysis your team needs. A Six who feels heard becomes your most loyal, thorough collaborator.
- **Type 7:** Channel their energy, don't suppress it. Give variety within structure. But hold them accountable for completion. A Seven who finishes something and gets recognized for the follow-through (not just the idea) will start to build that muscle.
- **Type 8:** Be direct. Don't sugarcoat. Show respect by being honest, and never go behind their back. If you disagree with an Eight, say so to their face. They'll respect you more for it than for any amount of diplomatic avoidance.
- **Type 9:** Ask for their opinion explicitly and then wait. The pause before a Nine speaks is not hesitation; it's them locating an opinion that's been buried under everyone else's. Create safe space for disagreement. Don't mistake their silence for agreement. It almost never is.

## Conflict Resolution

### Body Center Conflict

**Body Center conflict** is about autonomy and control. Eights confront directly, Ones correct through principles, and Nines withdraw to avoid the whole thing.

Picture this: an Eight director and a One manager disagree about how to handle an underperforming employee. The Eight says, "Fire them. They've had enough chances." The One says, "We need to follow the proper performance improvement process." Neither is wrong. But the Eight hears the One's insistence on process as weakness, and the One hears the Eight's decisiveness as recklessness. Meanwhile, the Nine team lead says nothing, privately agrees with the One, and goes home with a stomachache.

The key: acknowledge the anger underneath. Body types need to feel that their boundaries and autonomy are respected before they can soften. Start with "I hear you" before you start with "But."

### Heart Center Conflict

**Heart Center conflict** is about worth and recognition. Twos feel unappreciated, Threes feel exposed, and Fours feel misunderstood.

Picture this: a Two project coordinator, a Three team lead, and a Four creative director are on the same project. The project succeeds. The Three gets the credit in the company newsletter. The Two, who handled all the logistics that made the project possible, feels invisible. The Four, whose creative concept was the entire reason the project worked, feels like their contribution was reduced to a footnote. The Three doesn't understand why everyone isn't celebrating.

The key: validate the person before addressing the problem. Heart types need to feel seen before they can hear feedback. Saying "Your work mattered and here's specifically how" isn't flattery. It's the door that opens the conversation.

### Head Center Conflict

**Head Center conflict** is about safety and trust. Fives feel invaded, Sixes feel unsupported, and Sevens feel trapped.

Picture this: a Five engineer has been working on an architecture proposal for two weeks. A Six product manager asks to review it before it's "ready." The Five bristles (their work is being judged before it's complete). The Six is confused (they were just trying to stay informed so nothing goes wrong). A Seven designer jumps in with a completely different approach, and the Five feels like their two weeks of work just evaporated.

The key: provide clarity and predictability. Head types need to feel that the situation is understandable and that they have enough information before they can engage constructively. Surprises are not fun for this center. They're threats.

## Stress and Burnout Patterns

Burnout looks different for each type, and it often gets misdiagnosed because the symptoms don't match the standard "exhausted and disengaged" template. Here's what to watch for, and what to do when you see it.

**Type 1:** Burnout looks like relentless self-criticism and resentment that leaks into every interaction. The warning sign: everything feels wrong and no one is trying hard enough. Grace, our project manager, starts sending emails at midnight with increasingly sharp feedback. She's not suddenly mean; she's exhausted and her inner critic has taken the wheel. *What helps:* Tell her explicitly that her standards are valued. Then give her permission to let something be "good enough" this one time. She won't believe you the first time. Say it again.

**Type 2:** Burnout looks like martyrdom and emotional collapse. The warning sign: you're tracking every favor you've done and no one has reciprocated. Andre stops being warm and becomes brittle. He says things like, "I guess I'm the only one who cares about this team." *What helps:* Don't argue. Don't explain. Ask him, "What do you need right now?" The question itself may bring him to tears because nobody has asked in months.

**Type 3:** Burnout looks like emptiness behind the achievements. The warning sign: you've hit every goal and feel nothing. Wei delivers her quarterly results and immediately opens her laptop instead of celebrating. She can't stop because stopping means sitting with the void underneath the productivity. *What helps:* Create moments of genuine human connection that are completely decoupled from performance. Lunch with no agenda. A conversation about something that has nothing to do with work.

**Type 4:** Burnout looks like deep withdrawal and hopelessness. The warning sign: they've stopped creating and everything feels pointless. Soren cancels meetings, stops sharing work, and his Slack status stays gray for days. *What helps:* Don't try to cheer him up. Don't say "It's not that bad." Instead, acknowledge the difficulty genuinely: "This sounds really hard. I'm here when you want to talk about it." Then follow up in a few days without waiting for him to reach out. Fours in withdrawal need someone to come to them.

**Type 5:** Burnout looks like total detachment and cynicism. The warning sign: they've stopped caring about the work entirely and their contributions become mechanical. Yumi starts saying "Whatever you want" in meetings, which is her way of announcing she's checked out. *What helps:* Reduce demands on her energy. Give her a smaller, more focused scope. Ask what she's genuinely interested in. A Five who gets to go deep on one thing they care about can recover faster than one who's spread thin across ten things they don't.

**Type 6:** Burnout looks like spiraling anxiety and paralysis. The warning sign: they can't make a decision without imagining catastrophe, and their contingency planning has become the work itself. Darnell starts every sentence with "But what if..." and can't sign off on anything. *What helps:* Don't tell him to "just relax." Instead, reduce uncertainty concretely. Give him clear guardrails: "Here's exactly what happens if this doesn't work, and here's the backup plan." Structure calms a Six faster than reassurance.

**Type 7:** Burnout looks like frantic distraction and inability to be present. The warning sign: they're planning their next escape before the current project is done, and their usual enthusiasm has a manic edge. Lucia is pitching a new initiative every day and getting irritated when anyone asks about the old ones. *What helps:* Gently name the pattern: "I notice you're generating a lot of new ideas. Can we finish one thing before we start another?" Then make the finishing feel rewarding. Sevens aren't allergic to completion; they're allergic to the emptiness they expect on the other side of it.

**Type 8:** Burnout looks like rage and isolation. The warning sign: everyone around them feels like an obstacle or a threat, and their usual directness has hardened into hostility. Marcus stops delegating and starts doing everything himself because "nobody else can handle it." *What helps:* Match his directness. Don't tiptoe. Say, "You're burning out and it's affecting the team. What do you need to not do right now?" An Eight will respect honesty more than any amount of careful concern.

**Type 9:** Burnout looks like numbness and complete disengagement. The warning sign: they've stopped having opinions about anything, even things that matter to them, and they're physically present but energetically absent. Hana nods in every meeting but couldn't tell you what was discussed five minutes later. *What helps:* Don't ask "Are you okay?" (she'll say yes). Instead, ask a specific question that requires her actual opinion: "Hana, I want your honest take on this decision. Not what you think we want to hear, what *you* think." Then wait. The silence is where her opinion lives.

## Practice This

**The Type-Sharing Exercise.** In a team meeting, each person shares their type (or suspected type) and one thing they want others to know about how they work best. Keep it to two minutes per person. No debates about typing. No "I think you're actually a..." responses. The goal is understanding, not accuracy. A simple prompt: "When I'm at my best at work, I need ___. When I'm stressed, I tend to ___." Even people who don't know their type can answer those questions, and the answers will teach the team more than any personality report.

**The Center Check-In.** Before a big decision, pause and ask: "What does the Body Center say? What does the Heart Center say? What does the Head Center say?" In practice, this sounds like: "What does our gut tell us? (Body) How will this affect people and relationships? (Heart) What are the risks and unknowns we haven't addressed? (Head)" Even if your team doesn't have all three centers represented, this framework ensures you're considering action, feeling, *and* analysis. Write the three answers on a whiteboard. The missing perspective is almost always the one that would have prevented the next problem.

**The Conflict Protocol.** When tension arises between two people, each person answers three questions privately in writing before any discussion: "What am I feeling right now?" "What do I actually need?" "What story am I telling myself about the other person?" Then share the answers. Most conflicts dissolve, or at least transform, when people hear each other's honest answers. The story question is especially powerful: "I was telling myself you didn't respect my expertise" hits very differently from "You never listen to me."

**The Burnout Radar.** Once a month, each team member privately rates their energy on a 1-10 scale and shares one thing that would help. No justification needed. No performance-review consequences. This normalizes talking about stress before it becomes a crisis. Track the numbers over time. A steady decline from 7 to 5 to 3 is a signal that something structural needs to change, not just a person who needs a vacation.

**The Missing Center Audit.** Once a quarter, map your team by center. You don't need exact types; just note whether each person tends to lead with Body (action, instinct), Heart (relationships, image), or Head (analysis, strategy). Look at the balance. If a center is underrepresented, designate someone (rotating) to "hold" that perspective in key meetings. A Head-heavy team appoints a Heart advocate who asks: "How will this affect morale? Who haven't we consulted?" A Heart-heavy team appoints a Body advocate who asks: "What's the first concrete step? When do we start?"
