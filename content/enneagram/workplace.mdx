## The Enneagram at Work

You spend a staggering amount of your life at work. And most of the friction you experience there isn't about strategy, deadlines, or even competence. It's about people. The colleague who rewrites your email before sending it. The manager who needs to process every decision out loud. The teammate who disappears for two hours and comes back with a brilliant solution no one asked for.

The Enneagram doesn't put people in boxes at work. It opens a window into *why* your coworkers do what they do, and why you react the way you react. When you understand that your teammate's relentless follow-up emails aren't micromanaging but a Six's way of feeling safe, or that your boss's bluntness isn't aggression but an Eight's version of respect, everything shifts.

This isn't about labeling people over coffee. It's about building the kind of awareness that turns a dysfunctional team into one that actually leverages its differences. The most effective teams aren't the ones where everyone thinks alike. They're the ones where people understand *how* each person thinks, and make room for it.

## How Each Type Shows Up at Work

**Type 1: The Reformer.** You bring standards, integrity, and an eye for what could be better. Your work is thorough and principled, and people trust your quality. Your blind spot is rigidity: when "good enough" genuinely is good enough, you may struggle to let go. Perfectionism can slow you down and frustrate teammates who move faster.

**Type 2: The Helper.** You're the relational glue on any team. You notice who's struggling, you remember birthdays, and you make people feel seen. Your blind spot is overextending: you may say yes to everything, then feel resentful when no one notices your sacrifice. You sometimes struggle to advocate for your own ideas as fiercely as you advocate for others.

**Type 3: The Achiever.** You're the engine. You set goals, hit them, and make it look effortless. Teams love your energy and your ability to rally people around a vision. Your blind spot is confusing productivity with value. You can steamroll slower thinkers, cut corners on process, or avoid conversations that don't feel "efficient."

**Type 4: The Individualist.** You bring creativity, depth, and an ability to see what's missing that others overlook. Your work has a signature, a quality that's unmistakably yours. Your blind spot is taking things personally. Feedback can feel like rejection, and you may withdraw when the environment feels too impersonal or when your contributions go unacknowledged.

**Type 5: The Investigator.** You're the person who actually reads the whole report. You bring clarity, expertise, and calm analysis when everyone else is reacting. Your blind spot is hoarding, whether that's information, time, or energy. You may resist collaboration, share conclusions without showing your work, or disengage when meetings get emotional.

**Type 6: The Loyalist.** You're the one who spots the risks everyone else missed. Your loyalty and follow-through make you the backbone of any team. Your blind spot is worst-case thinking. You can slow momentum by raising too many concerns, and your need for reassurance can exhaust leaders who mistake it for lack of confidence.

**Type 7: The Enthusiast.** You bring energy, optimism, and an infectious sense of possibility. You're the brainstormer, the one who sees connections and opportunities. Your blind spot is follow-through. You may start more than you finish, resist boring-but-necessary tasks, and reframe problems so aggressively that you miss the actual issue.

**Type 8: The Challenger.** You bring decisiveness, directness, and a willingness to have the hard conversation no one else will. People know where they stand with you. Your blind spot is domination. You can take over meetings, dismiss quieter voices, and mistake vulnerability for weakness, both in yourself and others.

**Type 9: The Peacemaker.** You bring harmony, patience, and the ability to see every perspective. You're the natural mediator, the one who makes space for everyone. Your blind spot is self-erasure. You may go along with decisions you disagree with, avoid conflict until it explodes, and struggle to assert your own priorities.

## Team Dynamics by Center

**Body Center teams (8, 9, 1)** are action-oriented. They want to *do* something. Decisions get made quickly, and there's a bias toward movement. The shadow side: they can steamroll people who need more processing time, and unacknowledged anger simmers beneath the surface. If your team skews Body, build in reflection pauses before big decisions.

**Heart Center teams (2, 3, 4)** are relationally attuned. They care about how people feel, how things look, and whether the team is connected. The shadow side: they can get stuck in feelings, avoid hard truths to protect relationships, and spend too much energy on image. If your team skews Heart, create explicit permission for direct feedback.

**Head Center teams (5, 6, 7)** are analytical and strategic. They think before they act, anticipate problems, and generate ideas. The shadow side: analysis paralysis. They can overthink, over-plan, and delay action while chasing certainty that never comes. If your team skews Head, set deadlines for decisions and reward action, not just analysis.

## Managing Each Type

- **Type 1:** Give clear expectations and acknowledge their effort, not just results. They need to know their standards are respected.
- **Type 2:** Check in personally. Ask what *they* need. Recognize their contributions publicly.
- **Type 3:** Set clear goals and get out of their way. Offer honest feedback privately, and remind them that failure is data, not identity.
- **Type 4:** Honor their creative process. Give meaningful work, not busywork. Validate their feelings without getting lost in them.
- **Type 5:** Respect their need for preparation time. Send agendas in advance. Don't ambush them in meetings.
- **Type 6:** Be transparent about decisions and changes. Explain the "why." Don't dismiss their concerns as negativity.
- **Type 7:** Channel their energy, don't suppress it. Give variety, but hold them accountable for completion.
- **Type 8:** Be direct. Don't sugarcoat. Show respect by being honest, and never go behind their back.
- **Type 9:** Ask for their opinion explicitly. Create safe space for disagreement. Don't mistake their silence for agreement.

## Conflict Resolution

**Body Center conflict** is about autonomy and control. Eights confront directly, Ones correct through principles, and Nines withdraw to avoid the whole thing. The key: acknowledge the anger underneath. Body types need to feel that their boundaries are respected before they can soften.

**Heart Center conflict** is about worth and recognition. Twos feel unappreciated, Threes feel exposed, and Fours feel misunderstood. The key: validate the person before addressing the problem. Heart types need to feel seen before they can hear feedback.

**Head Center conflict** is about safety and trust. Fives feel invaded, Sixes feel unsupported, and Sevens feel trapped. The key: provide clarity and predictability. Head types need to feel that the situation is understandable before they can engage constructively.

## Stress & Burnout Patterns

- **Type 1:** Burnout looks like relentless self-criticism and resentment. Warning sign: everything feels wrong and no one is trying hard enough.
- **Type 2:** Burnout looks like martyrdom and emotional collapse. Warning sign: you're tracking every favor you've done and no one has reciprocated.
- **Type 3:** Burnout looks like emptiness behind the achievements. Warning sign: you've hit every goal and feel nothing.
- **Type 4:** Burnout looks like deep withdrawal and hopelessness. Warning sign: you've stopped creating and everything feels pointless.
- **Type 5:** Burnout looks like total detachment and cynicism. Warning sign: you've stopped caring about the work entirely.
- **Type 6:** Burnout looks like spiraling anxiety and paralysis. Warning sign: you can't make a decision without imagining catastrophe.
- **Type 7:** Burnout looks like frantic distraction and inability to be present. Warning sign: you're planning your next escape before the current project is done.
- **Type 8:** Burnout looks like rage and isolation. Warning sign: everyone around you feels like an obstacle or a threat.
- **Type 9:** Burnout looks like numbness and complete disengagement. Warning sign: you've stopped having opinions about anything, even things that matter to you.

## Practice This

**The Type-Sharing Exercise.** In a team meeting, each person shares their type and one thing they want others to know about how they work best. Keep it to two minutes per person. No debates about typing. The goal is understanding, not accuracy.

**The Center Check-In.** Before a big decision, pause and ask: "What does the Body Center say? What does the Heart Center say? What does the Head Center say?" Even if your team doesn't have all three centers represented, this framework ensures you're considering action, feeling, *and* analysis.

**The Conflict Protocol.** When tension arises, each person answers three questions before discussion: "What am I feeling right now?" "What do I actually need?" "What story am I telling myself about the other person?" Share the answers. Most conflicts dissolve when people hear each other's honest answers.

**The Burnout Radar.** Once a month, each team member privately rates their energy on a 1-10 scale and shares one thing that would help. No justification needed. This normalizes talking about stress before it becomes a crisis.
