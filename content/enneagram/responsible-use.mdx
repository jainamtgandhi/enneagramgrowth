## With Great Insight Comes Responsibility

The Enneagram is a powerful tool for self-understanding. Like any powerful tool, it can be used well or poorly. Here's how to use it well.

## Core Principles

### Share your own type — never assign someone else's.
Typing others without consent turns self-reflection into judgment. Even well-meaning type assignments can feel reductive and invasive.

### Hold your type loosely.
Your type describes patterns, not destiny. Use it as a lens for growth, not an excuse for behavior.

### Honor complexity.
No one is just their type. Culture, upbringing, health, and context all matter. Resist the urge to reduce people to numbers.

### This is not clinical.
The Enneagram is a framework for self-awareness, not a diagnostic tool. It does not replace therapy, counseling, or professional mental health support.

### Beware the "Enneagram ego."
It's easy to become attached to your type — to build an identity around it. The goal is to see through your patterns, not to perfect them.

## In Relationships

- Use the Enneagram to understand, not to win arguments
- "You're just doing your Three thing" is not a valid response in a disagreement
- Knowing someone's type doesn't mean you understand them — it means you have a starting point

## In Groups and Teams

- Never require type disclosure
- Don't use type to make hiring or assignment decisions
- Create space for people to share — or not share — their type journey

## The Bottom Line

The Enneagram's value lies in the compassion it generates — for yourself and others. If it's making you more judgmental, less curious, or more rigid, something has gone wrong. Return to the mirror.
