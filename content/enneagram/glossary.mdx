## Key Terms

### Arrow
A line connecting two types on the Enneagram symbol. Each type has a growth arrow (direction of integration) and a stress arrow (direction of disintegration).

### Body Center
The intelligence triad containing Types 8, 9, and 1. Associated with gut instinct and the core emotion of anger.

### Center of Intelligence
One of three triads (Body, Heart, Head) that group the nine types by their primary way of processing the world.

### Core Desire
The deep longing that each type is trying to fulfill. Where the core fear represents what a type runs from, the core desire represents what it runs toward (e.g., Type 1 desires integrity, Type 4 desires identity and significance).

### Core Fear
The fundamental fear that drives a type's behavior and defense mechanisms. Each type has a specific core fear.

### Core Motivation
The deep desire that underlies a type's actions and worldview. Understanding your core motivation is key to identifying your type.

### Counterphobic
A Six who responds to fear by confronting it directly rather than seeking safety. Not a separate type; a way Sixes can express their fear relationship.

### Countertype
Within each of the nine types, the one instinctual variant that looks most different from the other two. Countertypes can be harder to identify because their behavior doesn't match typical descriptions of their type.

### Fixation
The mental habit or cognitive pattern associated with each type: the repetitive thought loop that reinforces the type's worldview (e.g., resentment for Type 1, flattery for Type 2, vanity for Type 3). Related to but distinct from the Passion, which operates at the emotional level.

### Growth Direction (Integration)
The arrow pointing toward the type whose healthy qualities you access when growing. Moving in your growth direction represents expansion and development.

### Head Center
The intelligence triad containing Types 5, 6, and 7. Associated with analysis and the core emotion of fear.

### Heart Center
The intelligence triad containing Types 2, 3, and 4. Associated with emotional attunement and the core emotion of shame.

### Instinctual Variant (Subtype)
One of three biological drives, self-preservation, social, or one-to-one (sexual), that significantly influences how a type manifests.

### Levels of Development
A spectrum of health within each type, ranging from healthy to average to unhealthy. Describes how the same core motivation can manifest at different levels of consciousness.

### Mistyping
Identifying with the wrong Enneagram type. Common and normal, especially early in the self-discovery process.

### Passion
In Enneagram tradition, the emotional habit or "deadly sin" associated with each type (e.g., pride for Type 2, avarice for Type 5). Distinct from center emotions; for example, the Body Center's shared emotion is anger, but the specific Passion of Type 1 is resentment.

### Stress Direction (Disintegration)
The arrow pointing toward the type whose unhealthy qualities you access under stress. Recognizing your stress patterns is key to self-awareness.

### Triad
A group of three types that share a center of intelligence. Same as "center."

### Virtue
The positive quality that emerges when a type is operating at its healthiest (e.g., serenity for Type 1, humility for Type 2).

### Stacking
See *Instinctual Variant*. Refers to the order in which the three instinctual drives (SP, SO, SX) are prioritized. For example, SP/SX means self-preservation is dominant, sexual is secondary, and social is the blind spot.

### Wing
One of the two types adjacent to your core type on the Enneagram circle. Your wing adds a "flavor" to your core type without changing its fundamental nature.
