## Why Terminology Matters

The Enneagram has its own vocabulary, and knowing these terms makes everything else on this site (and in Enneagram books and conversations) click into place faster. Think of this page as your reference card. You don't need to memorize it; just come back whenever you hit a word that doesn't quite land.

Some terms below are straightforward. Others carry layers of meaning that entire books have been written about. We've kept the definitions practical, with brief examples to show how each concept shows up in real life.

---

## Core Concepts

### Center of Intelligence

One of three triads (Body, Heart, Head) that group the nine types by their primary way of processing the world. Your center is the first thing worth identifying, because it narrows nine types down to three. See [Centers](/enneagram/centers) for the full exploration.

### Body Center

The intelligence triad containing Types 8, 9, and 1. Associated with gut instinct, physical knowing, and the core emotion of anger. Even a Nine who never raises their voice has a deep, often hidden relationship with anger; it's the emotion this center manages, suppresses, or redirects. A Body Center person making a decision often says, "It just felt right in my gut."

### Heart Center

The intelligence triad containing Types 2, 3, and 4. Associated with emotional attunement, identity, and the core emotion of shame. Heart Center types are tuned to how they're perceived, whether they seek approval (Two), admiration (Three), or uniqueness (Four). When a Three feels the sting of a public failure, that's the Heart Center's shame surfacing.

### Head Center

The intelligence triad containing Types 5, 6, and 7. Associated with analysis, mental frameworks, and the core emotion of fear. Head Center types process the world by thinking about it first. A Six who mentally rehearses every possible outcome before a meeting is running on Head Center intelligence.

### Core Fear

The fundamental fear that drives a type's behavior and defense mechanisms. This is the fear you organize your life around avoiding, often without realizing it. Each type has a specific one. For example, a Two's core fear is being unloved or unwanted; a Five's is being helpless, depleted, or overwhelmed.

### Core Desire

The deep longing that each type is trying to fulfill. Where the core fear represents what a type runs from, the core desire represents what it runs toward. A One desires integrity and correctness. A Four desires a sense of identity and significance. The desire is the positive mirror of the fear.

### Core Motivation

The deep, often unconscious engine that underlies a type's actions and worldview. This is what separates the Enneagram from behavioral systems: two people can do the exact same thing for entirely different reasons. Understanding your core motivation is the key to identifying your type correctly.

### Triad

A group of three types that share a center of intelligence. Same as "center." You'll see both terms used interchangeably throughout the Enneagram literature.

### Wing

One of the two types adjacent to your core type on the Enneagram circle. Your wing adds a "flavor" to your core type without changing its fundamental motivation. A Six with a Seven wing (6w7) will look different from a Six with a Five wing (6w5), but both are still Sixes at their core. See [Wings](/enneagram/wings) for more detail.

## Dynamic Concepts

### Arrow

A line connecting two types on the Enneagram symbol. Each type has two arrows: one pointing toward its growth direction and one toward its stress direction. These lines describe how types shift under different conditions. See [Arrows](/enneagram/arrows) for the full picture.

### Growth Direction (Integration)

The arrow pointing toward the type whose healthy qualities you access when growing. Moving in your growth direction represents expansion and development. For example, a One integrating toward Seven becomes more spontaneous, playful, and open to imperfection.

### Stress Direction (Disintegration)

The arrow pointing toward the type whose unhealthy qualities you access under stress. Recognizing your stress patterns is one of the most practical things the Enneagram offers. When a normally steady Five starts to look scattered and impulsive, they may be disintegrating toward their Seven stress point.

### Levels of Development

A spectrum of health within each type, ranging from healthy to average to unhealthy. Describes how the same core motivation can manifest at different levels of consciousness and self-awareness. A healthy Two is genuinely generous; an average Two keeps score; an unhealthy Two manipulates through guilt. Same type, vastly different expressions.

### Instinctual Variant (Subtype)

One of three biological drives, self-preservation, social, or one-to-one (sexual), that significantly influences how a type manifests. Your instinctual variant answers *where* your type's energy goes. A self-preservation Seven focuses on comfort and planning; a social Seven focuses on group energy and sacrifice (this is the Seven countertype, and it can look very unlike a typical Seven).

### Stacking

The order in which the three instinctual drives (SP, SO, SX) are prioritized. For example, SP/SX means self-preservation is dominant, sexual (one-to-one) is secondary, and social is the blind spot. Your blind spot instinct is often the area of life where you feel least competent or most avoidant. See [Instincts](/enneagram/instincts) for more.

### Countertype

Within each of the nine types, the one instinctual variant that looks most different from the other two. Countertypes can be harder to identify because their behavior contradicts typical type descriptions. The social Four, for instance, internalizes suffering rather than expressing it, and can look more like a One or a Two on the surface.

### Counterphobic

A term used specifically for Sixes who respond to fear by confronting it directly rather than seeking safety. Not a separate type; rather, a way Sixes can express their relationship with fear. A counterphobic Six may look aggressive and bold, which leads to frequent [mistyping](/enneagram/mistyping) as an Eight.

## Traditional and Advanced Concepts

### Fixation (Fixation Point)

The mental habit or cognitive pattern associated with each type: the repetitive thought loop that reinforces the type's worldview. Think of it as the lens your mind defaults to. The One's fixation is resentment (scanning for what's wrong). The Two's is flattery (reading others' needs to earn love). The Three's is vanity (constant self-monitoring for how they're being perceived). Related to but distinct from the Passion, which operates at the emotional level.

### Passion

In Enneagram tradition, the emotional habit or "deadly sin" associated with each type. This is the automatic emotional response that fuels your type's pattern. The full set: anger/resentment (One), pride (Two), vanity/deceit (Three), envy (Four), avarice (Five), fear/doubt (Six), gluttony (Seven), lust/excess (Eight), sloth/self-forgetting (Nine). Distinct from center emotions; for example, the Body Center's shared emotion is anger, but the specific Passion of Type Nine is sloth (not laziness, but a forgetting of one's own priorities and desires).

### Virtue

The positive quality that emerges when a type is operating at its healthiest, essentially the antidote to the Passion. When a One releases their grip on resentment, the Virtue of serenity arises. When a Two lets go of pride, genuine humility takes its place. The Virtues are not achievements to pursue but natural qualities that surface when the Passion loosens its hold.

### Holy Idea

A higher cognitive perspective associated with each type in the Enneagram's spiritual traditions (particularly Ichazo and Almaas). Where the Fixation is the distorted mental pattern, the Holy Idea is the undistorted perception that the type is trying to recover. For example, the Six's Fixation is doubt; its Holy Idea is Holy Faith, the direct experience that support exists within reality itself. These are contemplative concepts; you don't need them to use the Enneagram practically, but they deepen the map considerably.

### Mistyping

Identifying with the wrong Enneagram type. Common and normal, especially early in the self-discovery process. Most people misidentify at least once, and the reasons are instructive. See [Common Misidentifications](/enneagram/mistyping) for a detailed guide.
