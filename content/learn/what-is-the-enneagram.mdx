## A Map, Not a Box

The Enneagram is a system of nine interconnected personality types. But calling it a "personality test" would be like calling a cathedral a building — technically accurate, but missing the point.

The Enneagram describes nine fundamental patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving. Each type reflects a core motivation — what drives you at the deepest level — and a core fear that shapes how you move through the world.

## Where Did It Come From?

The Enneagram's roots are debated. Some trace it to Sufi mysticism, others to early Christian contemplative traditions, and still others to the work of G.I. Gurdjieff, Oscar Ichazo, and Claudio Naranjo in the 20th century. What's clear is that it draws from multiple wisdom traditions and has been refined through decades of psychological observation.

It is not a clinical tool. It is not a substitute for therapy. It is a framework for self-awareness.

## How Is It Different?

Unlike most personality systems (Myers-Briggs, DISC, Big Five), the Enneagram:

- Focuses on **motivation**, not behavior
- Maps **growth and stress** paths (arrows)
- Includes **wings** that shade each type
- Describes **levels of health** within each type
- Is inherently **dynamic** — you are not a static label

## What It's Not

The Enneagram is not:
- A diagnosis
- A verdict on who you are
- A tool for labeling others
- A party trick or icebreaker
- A substitute for professional support

## What It Can Be

When used with care, the Enneagram can be:
- A mirror that shows you your patterns without judgment
- A language for understanding why you do what you do
- A bridge between you and people who seem nothing like you
- A compass for personal growth
